Transition from Classical to Statistical Mechanics#

Classical Mechanics#

We define Lagrangian (\(L\))
$\(L = T - V = \frac{m}{2}\dot{q}^2 - V(q,t)\)$

Principle of L.A.\(\rightarrow\) Classical particle path follows min action path.

\[\text{Action } S = \int_{t_1}^{t_2} L(\dot{q}, q, t) dt\]
\[\delta S = S[\bar{q}+\delta q] - S[\bar{q}] = 0\]
\[ \begin{align}\begin{aligned}\begin{split}\begin{align*} S[q + \delta q] &= \int_{t_1}^{t_2} L(\dot{q} + \delta \dot{q}, q + \delta q) dt\\ & \text{By Taylor expansion \& discarding higher order terms}\\ &= \int_{t_1}^{t_2} L(\dot{q},q,t) + \delta \dot{q}\frac{\partial L}{\partial \dot{q}} + \delta q \frac{\partial L}{\partial q} dt\\ &= S[q] + \int_{t_1}^{t_2} \delta \dot{q}\frac{\partial L}{\partial \dot{q}} + \delta q \frac{\partial L}{\partial q} dt\\ & \boxed{\frac{d}{dt}\frac{\partial L}{\partial \dot{q}} - \frac{\partial L}{\partial q} = 0} \hspace{50pt} \text{Lagrange's equation}\\ \end{align*}$$ The last equation is obtained by integration by parts. Notice there are no variation at both end. This is left as exercise.\ Familiar equation $F = ma$ can be recovered from Lagrange's equation\ $m\frac{d^2q}{dt^2} - \frac{\partial V}{\partial q} = 0$\end{split}\\\begin{split}$$\begin{align*} L & = T-V\\ &= \frac{1}{2} m\underset{vel}{\underset{\uparrow}{\dot{x}}}^2- \frac{1}{2}kx^2 \end{align*}\end{split}\end{aligned}\end{align} \]
\[\begin{split}\begin{align*} \frac{d}{dt}\frac{\partial L}{\partial\dot{q}} - \frac{\partial L}{\partial q} &= \frac{d}{dt} m\dot{x} + kx\\ &= m\ddot{x} + kx = 0 \\ & \text{ \hspace{20pt} } \Downarrow \\ & \text{ } \ddot{x} + \left(\frac{k}{m}\right) x = 0 \end{align*}\end{split}\]

Available solution

\[x(t) = a \cos(\omega t) + b \sin(\omega t)\]

where \(\omega = \sqrt{\frac{k}{m}}\).

Transistion to Statistical Mechanics#

Let’s consider \(N\) particle system.

Any variations can be described by the

set of coordinates. Each one of these are special and these are called microstate.

\[\begin{split}\begin{array}{lll} & \hspace{5pt} (x,y,z) & \hspace{5pt}(p_x, p_y, p_z) \\ & \hspace{5pt}\nearrow & \hspace{5pt}\nearrow \\ microstates := (r^N, p^N) = & (\underbrace{r_1, r_2, \cdots, r_N}_{\text{position}}; & \underbrace{p_1, p_2, \cdots, p_N}_{\text{momenta}}) \end{array}\end{split}\]

Collection of microstates span \(6N\) dimensional space called

“Phase Space”

+:———————————+:——————————–:+ | The uncertainty principle | ————————— | | \(\Rightarrow\) | We cannot know both | | | \(r\) & \(p\) simultaneously. | | | ————————— | +———————————-+———————————-+ | | \(\Downarrow\) | +———————————-+———————————-+ | | there must be an elemental | +———————————-+———————————-+ | | “volume” that can be | +———————————-+———————————-+ | | occupied by a unique | +———————————-+———————————-+ | | “state”. | +———————————-+———————————-+ | | \(\Updownarrow\) | +———————————-+———————————-+ | | Average volume for single | | | particle \((r,p)\) | +———————————-+———————————-+ | | is \(h^3\). | +———————————-+———————————-+ | | \(\nwarrow\) Plank constant. | +———————————-+———————————-+ | | see apendix. | +———————————-+———————————-+

Classical mechanics can, in principle, predict the future state of a system if all \(6N\) phase–space coordinates (positions and momenta) and the equations of motion are known. However, for a chemical system containing on the order of one mole of particles, tracking all microscopic variables is practically impossible. Instead, in chemistry and statistical mechanics, we describe systems using macroscopic variables such as average energy, temperature, and pressure. A macrostate is defined by these macroscopic quantities, while each macrostate corresponds to a large number of distinct microstates that share the same macroscopic properties. (In statistical mechanics, we count the number of microstates associated with a given macrostate. A macrostate with a larger number of microstates is more likely to be observed.)

This approach is based on the ergodic hypothesis, which states that, over a sufficiently long time, a system evolving according to classical mechanics spends time in a region of phase space proportional to the volume of that region. Consequently, time averages along a trajectory are equivalent to ensemble averages over phase space.\

Ensemble#

Ensemble is a large collection of a system (M systems in the ensemble) such that\

[Case 1]{.underline} Microcanonical Ensemble (NVE)
The system is isolated: the total energy \(E\), volume \(V\), and particle number \(N\) are all fixed. Because the system cannot exchange energy or particles with the surroundings, every accessible microstate with the same total energy is equally probable in thermodynamic equilibrium. This ensemble is conceptually useful when visualizing a perfectly isolated system.
[Case 2]{.underline} Canonical Ensemble (NVT)
Here, \(N\) and \(V\) are fixed, but the system is allowed to exchange energy with a much larger heat bath at temperature \(T\). As a result, the system’s energy fluctuates around an average value \(<E>\). The heat bath is so large that its temperature remains constant even when it exchanges energy with the system. To visualize the ensemble, imagine millions of independent copies of the system, each embedded in an enormous heat bath. The total energy of “system + bath” is fixed, but the system alone has fluctuating energy.
[Case 3]{.underline} NPT Ensemble
Here, \(N\) and \(P\) are fixed, but the system is allowed to exchange energy with a much larger heat bath at temperature \(T\). As a result, the system’s energy fluctuates around an average value \(<E>\). The heat bath is so large that its temperature remains constant even when it exchanges energy with the system. To visualize the ensemble, imagine millions of independent copies of the system, each embedded in an enormous heat bath. The total energy of “system + bath” is fixed, but the system alone has fluctuating energy.\

[Case 4]{.underline} Grand Canonical Ensemble
The assembly of all microstates with fixed \(V\). The energy & # of each chemical (molecule A \(\uparrow\) molecule B \(\downarrow\)) can fluctuate, however and the system is kept at equilibrium by being in contact with a heat bath at temp \(T\).
Generally, canonical ensemble is referred as \(NVT\) ensemble. For the biological system, we often uses \(NPT\) ensemble, and canonical ensemble are usually referred as \(\mu VT\) ensemble. In complex modeling for biological process, \(\mu PT\) ensemble is often used.
