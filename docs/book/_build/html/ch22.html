Chemical Equilibrium#

Macroscopic Approach#

Consider the rxn.

\[a A(g) + b B(g) \longrightarrow c C(g) + d D(g)\]
\[\begin{split}\begin{array}{ll} n_A = n_{A_0} - a \delta & dn_A = -ad\delta \\ \\ n_B = n_{B_0} - b \delta & dn_B = -bd\delta \\ \\ n_C = n_{C_0} - c \delta & dn_C = -cd\delta \\ \\ n_D = n_{D_0} - d \delta & dn_D = -dd\delta \\ \\ \end{array}\end{split}\]
\[\begin{split}\begin{array}{rl} dG &= -SdT+VdP+\mu_Adn_A + \mu_Bdn_B +\mu_Cdn_C + \mu_Ddn_D\\ & \downarrow \text{At const $T$ \& $P$}\\ &=(-\mu_Aa-\mu_Bb+\mu_Cc+\mu_Dd)d\delta\\ &=\parfracii{G}{\delta}{T}{P}d\delta \end{array}\end{split}\]
\[\begin{split}\begin{align*} \Delta G_r := \parfracii{G}{\delta}{T}{P} &= (\mu_Cc+\mu_Dd-\mu_Aa-\mu_Bb)\\ & \text{using the chemical potential for ideal gas}\\ &= c\mu_C^{\ominus}(T)+d\mu_D^{\ominus}-a\mu_A^{\ominus}-b\mu_B^{\ominus}\\ & \text{\hspace{10pt}} +RT\left(c\ln\frac{P_C}{P_0}+d\ln \frac{P_D}{P_0} - a\ln \frac{P_A}{P_0} -b\ln \frac{P_B}{P_0}\right) \end{align*}\end{split}\]

note that \(P_o\) is unity regardless of the unit we choose(i.e. \(P_o=1\)ATM)

\(\Delta_r G = \Delta_r G^{\ominus} + RT\ln Q\)
where

\[\Delta_r G^{\ominus}(T) = c\mu_C^{\ominus}(T)+d\mu_D^{\ominus}(T)-a\mu_A^{\ominus}(T)-b\mu_B^{\ominus}(T)\]

\(Q = \frac{ \left(\frac{P_C}{P_0}\right)^{c}\left(\frac{P_D}{P_0}\right)^{d}} {\left(\frac{P_A}{P_0}\right)^{a}\left(\frac{P_B}{P_0}\right)^{b}}\)
At eq,

Importance of \(\Delta G_r^{\ominus}(T) = -RT\ln K_P(T)\) can not be emphasized enough. This formula may shows up in general chemistry class as \(K_P(T)= e^{\frac{-\Delta G_r^{\ominus}}{RT}}\)
\(\star\) Le Chalelier’s Principle in Action
Consider a chemical reaction i gas phase. Just as in general chemistry, \(K_P\) denote the equilibrium constant. (\(K_P\) is expressed in terms of pressure)\

PCl\(_5\) \(\rightleftharpoons\) PCl\(_3\) + Cl\(_2\)

\[K_P(T) = \frac{P_{\text{PCl}_3} P_{\text{Cl}_2}}{P_{\text{PCl}_5}}\]

Starting 100% PCl\(_5\),

\[\begin{split}\begin{array}{lll} P_{\text{Cl}_2} &= P_{\text{PCl}_3} &=\frac{\delta}{1+\delta}P\\ P_{\text{PCl}_5} & &=\frac{1-\delta}{1+\delta}P \end{array}\end{split}\]
\[K_P(T) = \frac{\delta^2}{1-\delta^2}P\]

Because \(K_P(T)\) is a pure function of \(T\),
as total \(P\) changes, \(\frac{\delta^2}{1-\delta^2}\) must counteract
to keep it constant.
This is an example of Le Chatelier’s Principle.

If we are interested in expressing K in terms of concentration( \(K_C\) ) for gas phase reaction\

where \(K_C = \frac{\left(\frac{C_C}{C_0}\right)^{c}\left(\frac{C_D}{C_0}\right)^{d}}{\left(\frac{C_A}{C_0}\right)^{a}\left(\frac{C_B}{C_0}\right)^{b}}\)
Similar to the argument we mede before, \(C_o\) is the unit of concentration we choose to work with. Again, equilibrium constant is unit less.

\(\star\) Activity
Consider \(l-g\) equilibrium,
$\(\mu(g) = \mu(l)\)$

Gas mixture
\(\mu_i(g) = \mu_i^{\ominus}(g) + RT\ln \frac{P_i}{P_0}\) where \(P_i\) is partial pressure and \(P_0 = 1\) bar.
Using similar idea, we define (for liquid mixture),

\[\mu_i(l) = \mu_i^*(l) + RT\ln a_i\]

where \(a_i\) is activity

by setting \(a = 1\) for pure liquid at 1 bar \((P_0=f_i\times P_i^*)\) and \(a_i=\frac{f_iP_i^*}{P_0}\).
\(\mu_i(g)=\mu_i^{\ominus}(g) + RT\ln \frac{P_i^\ast}{P^0} = \mu_i^{\ominus}(l)\) where \(P_i^\ast\) is the vapor pressure of pure liquid.\#

\(\Rightarrow RT\ln \frac{P_i}{P_0} - RT\ln \frac{P^*_i}{P_0} = RT\ln a_i\).

\[\underset{x_i \cdots \text{mole fraction in the liquid}}{\underset{\rotatebox{90}{=}}{\frac{P_i}{P_i^\ast}}} = a_i\]

For liquid, chemical potential is defined as
$\(\mu_i(l) = \mu_i^*(l) + RT\ln a_i \hspace{50pt} \text{where } a_i=x_i\)\( The concept of activity is often encountered in area of analytical chemistry. please read standard analytical chemistry textbook if you are interested.\ Alternatively, we can consider reaction in ideal liquid mixture. We have an expression for a chemical potential for pure liquid\ \)\(\mu^*_A=\mu^{\ominus}_A(g)+k_BT\ln{P^*_A}\)\( With previous derivation,\ \)\(\mu_A(l)=\mu^{\ominus}_A(g)+k_BT\ln{P^*_A}+k_BT\ln{x_A}\)\( Note that this follows from Raoult's law(mole fraction in liquid)\ \)\(\mu_A(l)=\mu^{\ominus}_A(g)+k_BT\ln{P^*_A}+k_BT\ln{\frac{V_{liqMix}[A]}{N_{tot}}}\)\( Therefore concentration shows up in the above expression coresponds to liquid phase.\ If all vapor pressure of the chemcal are similar(\)P^_A=P^_B=P^_C=P^_D\(), mole fraction found in gas phase should be equal to that of liquid phase. In other words, we can replace the eqression to partial volume occupied by the component A as well as total mole of gas.\ \)\(\mu_A(l)=\mu^{\ominus}_A(g)+k_BT\ln{P^*_A}+k_BT\ln{\frac{V_{tot(g)}[A]}{N_{tot(g)}}}\)\( Now, repeat the same procedure used in \)K_P\( and putting first two terms into \)\Delta G^{\ominus}_r\(, we can deduce the following at equilibrium.\ \)\(\begin{align*} \Delta G^{\ominus}_r&=-k_BT\ln{\left[\frac{[C]^c[D]^d}{[A]^a[B]^b}\left(\frac{V_{tot(g)}}{N_{tot(g)}}\right)^{cd-ab}\right]}\\ & =-k_BT\ln{\left[\frac{[C]^c[D]^d}{[A]^a[B]^b}\left(\frac{RT}{P_o}\right)^{cd-ab}\right]}\\ \end{align*}\)\( If any of the chemical components are gas, the gas in the container is dominated by the components. So ignoring small vapor pressure, we obtain the familior general chemistry expression,\ \)\(\Delta G^{\ominus}_r =-k_BT\ln{\left[\frac{[C]^c[D]^d}{[A]^a[B]^b}\left(\frac{RT}{P_o}\right)^{\delta n}\right]}\)\( where \)\delta n$ represents the change in the number of mol of gas.\
