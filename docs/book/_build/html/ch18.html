Maxwell Relation, Basic Thermodynamics Process, Second and Third Laws of Thermodynamics#

Maxwell Relation#

So far we have \(U\), \(H\), \(A\), \(G\) state function of energy.
Because total derivative of any of these are

exact, we can use mathematical technique called “Maxwell relation”.\

\[\begin{split}\begin{array}{ll}dA &= \left(\frac{\partial A}{\partial V}\right)_TdV + \left(\frac{\partial A}{\partial T}\right)_VdT \cdots \text{total}\\ dA &= -PdV - SdT \end{array}\end{split}\]

then

\[\begin{split}\begin{array}{rcl} \begin{array}{l}\left(\frac{\partial A}{\partial V}\right)_T = -P\\ \left(\frac{\partial A}{\partial T}\right)_V = -S\end{array} & \overset{\text{exact}}{\Rightarrow} & \left(\frac{\partial P}{\partial T}\right)_V = \left(\frac{\partial S}{\partial V}\right)_T\\ & & \text{\hspace{30pt}} \downarrow\\ & & \text{ Maxwell relation } \end{array}$$ [HW]{.underline}\ Obtain all other Maxwell relation.\ $$\begin{array}{c|c|c} \text{Thermodynamic energy} & \text{Total diff} & \text{Maxwell relation}\\ & & \\ U & dU = TdS-PdV &\left(\frac{\partial T}{\partial V}\right)_S = -\left(\frac{\partial P}{\partial S}\right)_V \\ & & \\ H & dH = TdS + VdP & \left(\frac{\partial T}{\partial P}\right)_S = \left(\frac{\partial V}{\partial S}\right)_P\\ & & \\ A & dA = -SdT-PdV & \left(\frac{\partial S}{\partial V}\right)_T = \left(\frac{\partial P}{\partial T}\right)_V \\ & & \\ G & dG = -SdT+VdP & \left(\frac{\partial S}{\partial P}\right)_T = -\left(\frac{\partial V}{\partial T}\right)_P \end{array}\end{split}\]
\[\begin{split}\begin{align*} T =\left(\frac{\partial U}{\partial S}\right)_V = \left(\frac{\partial H}{\partial S}\right)_P\\ P =-\left(\frac{\partial U}{\partial V}\right)_S = -\left(\frac{\partial A}{\partial V}\right)_T\\ S = -\left(\frac{\partial A}{\partial T}\right)_V = -\left(\frac{\partial G}{\partial T}\right)_P\\ V = \left(\frac{\partial H}{\partial P}\right)_S = \left(\frac{\partial G}{\partial P}\right)_T \end{align*}\end{split}\]

Example of use of Maxwell relation
Compute \(\Delta S(T,V)\) at const \(T\) for ideal gas.
$\(\begin{align*} \Delta S &= \int_{V_1}^{V_2}\left(\frac{\partial S}{\partial V}\right)_TdV\\ & \downarrow \text{ Maxwell }\\ &= \int_{V_1}^{V_2}\left(\frac{\partial P}{\partial T}\right)_VdV\\ & \downarrow \text{ Ideal gas }\\ &=\int_{V_1}^{V_2}\frac{nR}{V}dV\\ &= nR \ln \frac{V_2}{V_1} \hspace{20pt} \text{ isothermal process } \end{align*}\)$

Example of Basic Thermodynamic Process#

We have already discussed that the internal energy \(U\) is a state function. We also showed that the natural variables of \(U\) are the entropy \(S\) and the volume \(V\), so that \(U = U(S,V)\) and $\(dU = T\, dS - P\, dV.\)\( Starting from this expression, we can use Maxwell relations to rewrite \)U\( in terms of different variables. In particular, we now derive an expression for \)U\( as a function of temperature \)T\( and volume \)V$.

To do this, we want to re-express \(dS\) in terms of \(dT\) and \(dV\). We consider entropy \(S\) as a function of \(T\) and \(V\): $\(S = S(T,V).\)\( Thus its total differential is \)\(dS = \left(\frac{\partial S}{\partial T}\right)_V dT + \left(\frac{\partial S}{\partial V}\right)_T dV.\)\( Substituting this into \)dU = T, dS - P, dV\( gives \)$dU = T\left(\frac{\partial S}{\partial T}\right)_V dT + T\left(\frac{\partial S}{\partial V}\right)_T dV

  • P, dV.$$

Using the definition of heat capacity {#using-the-definition-of-heat-capacity .unnumbered}#

From previous HW problem, the heat capacity at constant volume is $\(C_V = T\left(\frac{\partial S}{\partial T}\right)_V.\)\( Therefore, \)$dU = C_V, dT

  • T\left(\frac{\partial S}{\partial V}\right)_T dV

  • P, dV.$$

Using a Maxwell relation {#using-a-maxwell-relation .unnumbered}#

From the Helmholtz free energy, $\(A = U - TS, \qquad dA = -S\, dT - P\, dV,\)\( we obtain the Maxwell relation \)$\left(\frac{\partial S}{\partial V}\right)_T#

\left(\frac{\partial P}{\partial T}\right)_V.$\( Substituting this into our expression for \)dU\(, we obtain \)$dU = C_V, dT

  • T\left(\frac{\partial P}{\partial T}\right)_V dV

  • P, dV.$$

Thus, the general expression is $\(\boxed{ dU = C_V\, dT + \bigg[\, T\left(\frac{\partial P}{\partial T}\right)_V - P \bigg] dV. }\)$

Simplification for an ideal gas {#simplification-for-an-ideal-gas .unnumbered}#

For an ideal gas, $\(P = \frac{nRT}{V}.\)\( Therefore, \)\(\left(\frac{\partial P}{\partial T}\right)_V = \frac{nR}{V}, \qquad T\left(\frac{\partial P}{\partial T}\right)_V = \frac{nRT}{V} = P.\)$

Hence the bracketed term vanishes: $\(T\left(\frac{\partial P}{\partial T}\right)_V - P = 0.\)$

So for an ideal gas, $\(\boxed{ dU = C_V\, dT. }\)$

This result shows that the internal energy of an ideal gas depends only on its temperature and not on its volume. Let’s think this in terms of physics.
Internal energy (\(U\)) is the energy contained in the system (i.e. translations, rotations, vibrations of each molecule in the system). The internal energy is the energy of the whole system excluding the potential energy and kinetic enregy of the whole system. In other words, internal energy U does not depend on any variable associated with center of mass of the system. In fact, internal energy is a function of relative coordinates \(\left( x^{3N-3},p^{3N-3} \right)_{CM}\) of the particles cointained in the system. In general, internal energy often depends on volume as well as temperature. However, \(U\) for an ideal gas (translation, rotation, vibration) is not affected by other molecules because ideal gas does not possess any intermolecular interaction. Since ideal gas system is composed of completely independent molecules by definition, volume has no effect to those the energy contained in the system (i.e. translations, rotations, vibrations of each molecule in the system). In general, ideal gas are assumed to have zero vibrational \(\&\) rotational energies. Although there is no ideal gas found in nature, He gas is considered to be close to an ideal gas system.\

Work and Heat in a Cyclic Process#

To understand the behavior of the internal energy \(U\), as well as heat \(q\) and work \(w\), let us examine a simple cyclic process in the \(T\)–\(V\) plane. Assume the system is an ideal gas with constant heat capacity \(C_V = \frac{3}{2}k_B\) (monatomic ideal gas).

The cycle consists of four steps:

\[\begin{split}\begin{align*} &1.\ \textbf{Isothermal expansion} && (T_1, V_1) \rightarrow (T_1, V_2) && \text{(expansion at constant temperature)} \\ &2.\ \textbf{Isochoric heating} && (T_1, V_2) \rightarrow (T_2, V_2) && \text{(temperature increases at constant volume)} \\ &3.\ \textbf{Isothermal compression} && (T_2, V_2) \rightarrow (T_2, V_1) && \text{(compression at constant temperature)} \\ &4.\ \textbf{Isochoric cooling} && (T_2, V_1) \rightarrow (T_1, V_1) && \text{(temperature decreases at constant volume)} \end{align*}\end{split}\]

[HW]{.underline}
For each step, compute the work \(w\) and heat \(q\) transferred. Use the ideal gas law and the expressions $\(w = -\int P_{\mathrm{ext}}\, dV, \qquad dU = C_V\, dT \qquad (\text{ideal gas}).\)$

Expected Results {#expected-results .unnumbered}#

\[\begin{split}\boxed{ \begin{aligned} &\textbf{Step 1 (isothermal expansion)} \\ & w_1 = -n k_B T_1 \ln\!\left(\frac{V_2}{V_1}\right), \qquad q_1 = +n k_B T_1 \ln\!\left(\frac{V_2}{V_1}\right), \\[6pt] &\textbf{Step 2 (isochoric heating)} \\ & w_2 = 0, \qquad q_2 = \frac{3}{2} n k_B (T_2 - T_1), \\[6pt] &\textbf{Step 3 (isothermal compression)}\\ & w_3 = -n k_B T_2 \ln\!\left(\frac{V_1}{V_2}\right), \qquad q_3 = +n k_B T_2 \ln\!\left(\frac{V_1}{V_2}\right), \\[6pt] &\textbf{Step 4 (isochoric cooling)} \\ & w_4 = 0, \qquad q_4 = \frac{3}{2} n k_B (T_1 - T_2). \end{aligned} }\end{split}\]

[HW:]{.underline} Add up \(w_1 + w_2 + w_3 + w_4\) and \(q_1 + q_2 + q_3 + q_4\) over the entire cycle. Show that neither the total work nor the total heat is zero, even though the system returns to its initial state \((T_1, V_1)\).

Conclusion {#conclusion .unnumbered}#

At the end of the cycle, the system has returned to its original state, so its internal energy is unchanged: $\(\Delta U_{\text{cycle}} = 0.\)\( Therefore, from the first law of thermodynamics, \)\(\sum q_i + \sum w_i = 0.\)$

However, the individual values of \(\sum q_i\) and \(\sum w_i\) depend on the specific path taken in the \(T\)–\(V\) plane. A different cycle connecting the same starting and ending point would give different total heats and works.

\[\boxed{ \text{Since } q_{\text{cycle}} \text{ and } w_{\text{cycle}} \text{ depend on the path}, \quad q \text{ and } w \text{ are not state functions.} }\]

This example demonstrates that only \(U\) (and other thermodynamic potentials with natural variables) are state functions, while \(q\) and \(w\) are path dependent.

Second Law of Thermodynamics#

Up to this point, we have defined entropy only for reversible processes. For a reversible change, we showed $\(dS = \frac{\delta q_{\mathrm{rev}}}{T},\)\( and therefore \)S$ is a state function.

However, most real processes in nature are irreversible: friction, rapid expansion, heat flow across a finite temperature difference, and so on. During an irreversible process, the system is not in equilibrium, and describing such nonequilibrium states statistically is extremely difficult. Thus, we remain within classical thermodynamics and compare any real process to a hypothetical reversible path between the same initial and final equilibrium states.

Irreversible work is always larger in magnitude than reversible work

Consider the work term $\(w = -\int P_{\text{ext}}\, dV.\)\( For a reversible process, \)P_{\text{ext}}\( is always adjusted so that \)P_{\text{ext}} = P_{\text{sys}}\(. For an irreversible expansion, \)P_{\text{ext}} < P_{\text{sys}}\(, and the system does less (more negative) work in magnitude. Thus, \)\(w_{\mathrm{irr}} > w_{\mathrm{rev}} \quad \text{(expansion)}.\)$

A similar argument for compression (\(P_{\text{ext}} > P_{\text{sys}}\)) again yields $\(w_{\mathrm{irr}} > w_{\mathrm{rev}}.\)\( Therefore, for any process, \)\(w_{\mathrm{irr}} > w_{\mathrm{rev}}.\)$

Consequences for heat: the Clausius inequality

Because internal energy is a state function, $\(dU = w_{\mathrm{irr}} + q_{\mathrm{irr}} = w_{\mathrm{rev}} + q_{\mathrm{rev}}.\)\( Since \)w_{\mathrm{irr}} > w_{\mathrm{rev}}\(, the reversible path must transfer more heat: \)\(q_{\mathrm{rev}} > q_{\mathrm{irr}}.\)$

Using the definition of entropy for the reversible path, $\(dS = \frac{\delta q_{\mathrm{rev}}}{T},\)\( we obtain the **Clausius inequality** for irreversible processes: \)\(dS \ge \frac{\delta q}{T},\)$ with equality only for reversible processes.

Integrating around a cycle gives the engineering form of the Second Law: $\(\oint \frac{\delta q}{T} \le 0.\)$

Statement of the Second Law

For an isolated system (\(\delta q = 0\)), $\(dS_{\text{isolated}} > 0 \qquad \text{(irreversible process)}.\)$ Thus,

The entropy of an isolated system never decreases during a spontaneous process. It remains constant only for reversible processes.
