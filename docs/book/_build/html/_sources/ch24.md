Let $\tau$ and $l$ as a unit of time and length, respectively. Consider
a small box(cube) inside fluid. Particle suspended in the fluid can go
to left ($50\%$) or right($50\%$). Number of particles pasing through
the surface, as shown in the figure, can be obtained by setting the
number of particles entering from left for time period ($\Delta t$) as\
$$\frac{1}{2}N(x-\Delta x,t) \frac{\Delta t}{\tau}  \cdots \text{notice that unit is a pure number}$$
Similarly, the number of particle entering from right for time period
($\Delta t$) as\
$$\frac{1}{2}N(x+\Delta x,t)\frac{\Delta t}{\tau}$$ Therefore, net
particle movement through the surface is
$$-\frac{1}{2}N(x+\Delta x,t)\frac{\Delta t}{\tau}+  \frac{1}{2}N(x-\Delta x,t)\frac{\Delta t}{\tau}$$
When surface is thin enough($2\Delta x \Rightarrow 2l$ ), the
concentration can be expanded by Taylor series. Therefore, net movement
of particles at the surface becomes\
$$-\frac{1}{2}\left(N(x,t)+\frac{dN}{dx}l\right)\frac{\Delta t}{\tau}+  \frac{1}{2}\left(N(x,t)-\frac{dN}{dx}l\right)\frac{\Delta t}{\tau}=-\frac{dN}{dx}l\frac{\Delta t}{\tau}$$
Because $l=\Delta x =\Delta y= \Delta z$ and
$[conc]:=c=\frac{N}{{l}^3}=\frac{N}{V}$\
the concentration flux ($\frac{\# \text{of particle}}{V}$ per unit area
unit time)\
$$J=-\frac{1}{V}\frac{dN}{dx}\frac{\frac{\Delta t}{\tau}}{l\Delta t}=-\frac{1}{l\tau}\frac{dc}{dx}$$
denoting the constant as diffusion constant D $D=\frac{1}{l\tau}$, we
get\
$$J=-D\frac{dc}{dx}$$

But $J = cs$ where $c$ is concentration and $s$ is the speed of the
particle.

$$\begin{align*}
s = \frac{J}{c} & = -\frac{D}{c}\parfraci{c}{x}{T,P} \\
& = \frac{DF}{k_BT} \text{ from \circled{1}}
\end{align*}$$

Since $F_\text{fric} = f\cdot s$ and $f = 6\pi \eta r$\
where $\eta$ is viscosity and $r$ is radius of spherical particle,\
$D = \frac{k_BT}{f} \cdots$ Stoke-Einsten equation.\

$\star$ Convection\
Let $v$ be the velocity of fluid.\
$$\begin{align*}
J_\text{conv} &= \frac{c Av\Delta t}{A\Delta t} \leftarrow \text{ per unit area, unit } t\\
&= cv
\end{align*}$$

Assume $v$ is approximately constant between short distance.

Then,\
$$\begin{align*}
\frac{\partial c}{\partial t} &= \frac{\overset{\text{coming in}}{\overset{\downarrow}{J_c(x)}} - \overset{\text{coming out of slab}}{\overset{\downarrow}{J_c(x+\Delta x)}}}{\Delta x}\\
&= \frac{cv}{\Delta x} - \left\{c + \left(\frac{\partial c}{\partial x}\right)\Delta x\right\}\frac{v}{\Delta x}\\
&= -\left(\frac{\partial c}{\partial x}\right)v
\end{align*}$$

So, we can modify diffusion equation to moving fluid version.

$$\frac{\partial c}{\partial t} = D\frac{\partial^2 c}{\partial x^2}-v\frac{\partial c}{\partial x} \cdots \text{ Generalized diffusion equation}$$

Solution of the diffusion equation\
$$c(x,t) = \frac{n_0}{A(\pi D t)^{1/2}}e^{-x^2/4Dt} \leftarrow \text{ 1D }$$

$$c(x, t) = \frac{n_0}{A(\pi D t)^{3/2}}e^{-r^2/4Dt} \leftarrow \text{ 3D }$$

$\star$ Diffusion-Controlled reaction\
Consider\
$$A + B \underset{k_r}{\overset{k_d}{\rightleftharpoons}} AB \overset{k_b}{\longrightarrow} P$$

where A + B $\rightarrow$ AB is limitted by diffusion.\
Using the steady state approximation,

$$\frac{d(AB)}{dt} = k_dAB - k_r(AB) - k_b(AB) = 0$$

$$(AB) = \frac{k_dAB}{k_b+k_r}$$

So,

$$\frac{dP}{dt} = k_b[AB] = k_{tot}[A][B]$$

where $k_{tot} = \frac{k_bk_d}{k_b+k_r}$.

::: center
+:--------------------------------------------------------------------------------+:------------------------------+
|   ----------------------------------------------------------------------------- |   --------------------------- |
|   Diffusion controlled limit $k_r<<k_b$.                                        |   Note: It can be shown       |
|   $k_{tot}\simeq k_d$                                                           |   $k_d = \frac{8RT}{3\eta}$   |
|   Activation controlled limit $k_b<<k_r$.                                       |   $R$ is gas constant         |
|   $k_{tot}\simeq k_bk_\text{eq} \text{ where } k_\text{eq} = \frac{k_d}{k_r}$   |   --------------------------- |

$\star$ The Material Balance Equation (Core topic of Chemical
Engineering)\
We have seen

$$\frac{\partial c}{\partial t} = D\frac{\partial ^2 c}{\partial x^2} - v \frac{\partial c}{\partial x} \cdots \text{ General diffusion equation}$$

Assuming first order reaction,\
$$\frac{\partial c}{\partial t} = -k_r c$$

Combine to get material balance equation,

$$\frac{\partial c}{\partial t} = D\frac{\partial ^2 c}{\partial x^2} - \nu\frac{\partial  c}{\partial x}-k_r c$$
