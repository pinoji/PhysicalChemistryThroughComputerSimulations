Reaction Dynamics and Kinetics#

“Reaction Dynamics” is a branch of physical chemistry which deals with the motion of the system under the action of forces. This microscopic study is often done in a computer simulation (quantum mechanically or classically) and tracking motion of particles in the system (the system could contain a large number of particles) can be quite tedious. In this field of chemistry, many simulations are performed by combining trajectory calculation and probability theory. However, time windows of a simulation are often very short because a computational resource is limited. Collision theory is useful because one can count the number of chances that chemical reaction could happen without tracking all particle motion in the system. “Reaction Kinetics” is a study of a rate of reactions. Kinetics can be used to identify the reaction mechanism as well as some information on transition state. Both Dynamics and Kinetics are used to predict how fast the reaction proceeds in given conditions.\

Temperature Dependence of Reaction Rate

For many reactions,
$\(A + B \longrightarrow AB^\ddagger \longrightarrow C\)$

A and B have to collide to make AB\(^\ddagger\).
Let \(Z\) be the frequency of collision (how often?) of A and B and \(\rho\) be the steric factor for orientation dependent reaction.
Then, the Arrhenius equation is rewritten by $\(k = Ae^{-E_a/RT}\)\( or\ \)\(=\underset{Z\rho}{\underset{\rotatebox{90}{=}}{A}}e^{-E_a/k_BT}\)$

$Ea\( is called activation energy and it is the barrier height for the forward reaction. Typical value of \)E_a$ range from 50 to 200 kJ/mol.\

Example: To double the rate for some reaction at \(T = 310K \cdots\) Body Temperature and assume \(E_a=100 kJ/mol\) for this reaction.

\[2 = \frac{k_2}{k_1} = \frac{\cancel{A}e^{-E_a/RT_2}}{\cancel{A}e^{-E_a/RT_1}}\]

You should get 315K.

Justification of Arrihenius Equation (intuitive version)

In order for a reaction to happen, reactant A and B must be close to each other. We call this special form of reactant as “AB complex”. The probability of forming AB complex is usually determined by collision theory and relate to pre-exponential factor in the Arrhenius Equation. The only part of AB complex (energy of the complex must be higher than \(E_a\)) can become a product. At the top (the saddle point) of the potential energy surface along the reaction coordinate, Ab complex turns into transition state (TS) and could become a product. In transition state theory, any product formation must go through this transition state. At transition state, the reaction coordinate must possess at least \(E_a\). Subsequent energy randomization leads to the formation of products. Meanwhile, AB complex and transition state are assumed to be in static equilibrium (Steady state). Therefore, we can obtain the ratio of AB complex and TS by the statistical thermodynamics using state counting. To simplify the argument, we assume that all vibrational and rotational modes except one degree of freedom (the reaction coordinate) are shared by AB-complex and TS. By statistical mechanical concept, we can predict the ratio of reactant and product by counting the number of accessible states (number of ways to distribute the energy into each coordinate (modes)) for AB complex and TS. Since we can make all vibrational modes and rotational modes to be completely independent of each other, phase space volume can be computed as
$\(\text{ phase space of AB complex} :=V_AB= Q_{AB} \times R\)\( \)\(\text{ phase space of TS}:=V_{TS}= Q_{TS} \times R\)\( where Q represent the number of way to randomize energy in the reaction coordinate and R represent the one for the rest of the degree of freedom. Then, it follows that,\ \)\(\frac{[TS]}{[\text{AB complex}]}=\frac{V_{TS}}{V_{AB}}=\frac{Q_{TS}}{Q_{AB}}\)\( Now, assume both TS and AB complex obeys the Boltzmann distribution, we get\ \)\(Q_{TS}= \sum\limits_{i}\frac{e^{-(\epsilon_i+E_a)\beta}}{\sum\limits_{i}e^{-(\epsilon_i)\beta}}\)\( \)\(Q_{AB}= \sum\limits_{i}\frac{e^{-(\epsilon_i)\beta}}{\sum\limits_{i}e^{-(\epsilon_i)\beta}}\)\( Therefore, the ratio\ \)\(\frac{[TS]}{[\text{AB complex}]}=\frac{\sum\limits_{i}e^{-(\epsilon_i+E_a)\beta}}{\sum\limits_{i}e^{-(\epsilon_i)\beta}}=e^{-E_a\beta}\)\( Since the product concentration \)[\text{Prod}]\(, and the reactant concentration \)[\text{React}]\( is related bythe following formula,\ \)\([\text{Prod}]=k[TS]\)\( \)\(f[\text{React}]=[\text{AB complex}] \cdots \text{f relates to collision frequency}\)\( \)\(\frac{ [\text{Prod}]}{[\text{React}]}= Ae^{-E_a\beta}= Ae^{\frac{-E_a}{k_BT}} \cdots \text{pre-exponential factor \)A=kf\(}\)$

This ratio, \(\frac{ [\text{Prod}]}{[\text{React}]}\) is different from the corresponding thermodynamic ratio(equilibrium constant \(K\)) if there is a distinct TS along the reaction coordinates. The ratio will be identical to the thermodynamic ration if the reaction involves a barrierless process. In the exothermic reaction, reverse reaction becomes negligible, and RHS of the equation becomes a rate constant as desired for Arrhenius rate constant.\

\(P = \left\{1 + \frac{k_ae^{-k_bt}-k_be^{-k_at}}{k_b-k_a}\right\}A_0\)

\(\star\) Kinetic Control (One reactant, multiple outcomes)

\[\begin{split}\begin{align*} A \longrightarrow B & \hspace{50pt}\frac{dB}{dt} = k_1[A]\\ A \longrightarrow C & \hspace{50pt}\frac{dC}{dt} = k_2[A] \end{align*}\end{split}\]
\[\% B \text{ formed } = \frac{k_1}{k_1 + k_2} \text{ in general } \frac{k_i}{k_\text{tot}}\]

Product B or C can be favored depending on the condition.

At low T, Most of reactant do not have enough energy to overcome the barrior for the formation of product A. Therefore, Porduct B with low reaction barrior is favored. Thermodynamically more stable product A is not significantly formed at this temperature. It is important to note that almost no product can go back to reactant at this temperature because required energy in the reaction coordinate are tend to be absorbed by the heat bath.\

At high T, more reactant have enough energy to overcome the barrior for the formation of product A. Therefore, the increase in the formation of product A is observed by increasing the temperature of the system. Note that Product B is still produced.\

Enzymes#

$\star\( Enzymes \)\cdots$ Special protein or nucleic acid containing [an active site]{.underline} (bind to substrate, reactant and turn them into product).\

E $\cdots$ Enzyme S $\cdots$ Substrate P $\cdots$ Product
\[E + S \underset{k_r}{\overset{k_f}{\rightleftharpoons}} ES \overset{k_b}{\longrightarrow} P + E\]
\[\frac{dP}{dt} = k_b(ES)\]

Using S.S.,

\[\frac{d(ES)}{dt} = k_fE\cdot S-k_r(ES) - k_b(ES) = 0\]
\[(ES) = \frac{k_fE\cdot S}{k_r+k_b} = \frac{1}{k_M}E\cdot S \text{ where } k_M = \frac{k_r + k_b}{k_f}\]

by conservation law,

\[\begin{split}\begin{align*} E_0 = E + (ES) &= \frac{k_M(ES)}{S} + (ES)\\ &= (ES) \left\{1 + \frac{k_M}{S}\right\} \end{align*}\end{split}\]

Typically, \([S]>>E_0\) in biological environment (\([S]=[S]_0\))

then, $\((ES) = \frac{E_0}{1+\frac{k_M}{S_0}}\)$

\[Rate = \frac{dP}{dt} = k_b(ES) = \frac{k_bE_0}{1 + \frac{k_M}{S_0}} \cdots \text{ The Michaelis-Menton equation }\]

\(\begin{array}{l} k_M>0\\S_0>0\end{array}\) \(\underset{v_\text{max} = k_bE_0}{\underbrace{\frac{dP}{dt} \text{ is maximum}}_{\rotatebox{90}{=:}}}\) when \(S_0>>k_M\).

\[\frac{dP}{dt} = \frac{v_\text{max}}{1+\frac{k_M}{S_0}} \Rightarrow \frac{1}{Rate} = \frac{1}{v_\text{max}} + \left(\frac{k_M}{v_\text{max}}\right)\frac{1}{S_0}\]
