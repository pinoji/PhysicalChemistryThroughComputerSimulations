Free Energy, Pressure, \(\&\) Gas Laws#

Helmholtz Free Energy#

\[\begin{split}\begin{align*} S &= -k_B\sum \mathbb{P}_i\ln \mathbb{P}_i\\ & \text{Since $\ln {\mathbb{P}_i}=-(\beta E_i + \ln Z)$}\\ &= -k_B\sum \mathbb{P}_i(-\beta E_i - \ln Z)\\ &= k_B\beta <E> + k_B \ln Z\\ \end{align*}\end{split}\]

Because we established \(\beta=\frac{1}{k_BT}\), plug this back into expression of \(S\)
$\(\begin{align*} & S= \frac{E}{T} + k_B \ln Z \hspace{10pt} \\ &A=\underbrace{E - TS}_{\Downarrow} = -k_B T \ln Z = -\beta^{-1} \ln Z\\ &\text{Often denote as A and is called Helmholtz free energy.}\\ &\text{(This quantity is often used in thermodynamics.)} \end{align*}\)$

Gibbs Free Energy#

\[\begin{split}\begin{align*} S &= -k_B\sum \mathbb{P}_i\ln \mathbb{P}_i\\ & \text{Since $\ln {\mathbb{P}_i}=-(\beta E_i+ \beta PV_i+ \ln \Delta)$}\\ &= -k_B\sum \mathbb{P}_i(-\beta E_i -\beta PV_i - \ln \Delta)\\ &= k_B\beta (<E> +P<V>) + k_B \ln \Delta\\ \end{align*}\end{split}\]

Because we established \(\beta=\frac{1}{k_BT}\), plug this back into expression of \(S\)
$\(\begin{align*} & S= \frac{E+PV}{T} + k_B \ln \Delta \hspace{10pt} \\ &G=\underbrace{E - TS+PV}_{\Downarrow} = -k_B T \ln \Delta = -\beta^{-1} \ln \Delta\\ &\text{Often denote as G and is called Gibbs free energy.}\\ &\text{(This quantity is central in thermodynamics of biology.)} \end{align*}\)$

Enthalpy#

We can derive enthalpy starting from the statistical definition of entropy: $\(S = -k_B \sum_i \mathbb{P}_i \ln \mathbb{P}_i.\)$

Suppose the system can exchange energy with the surroundings, and also do pressure-volume work. For each microstate \(i\), the probability is $\(\mathbb{P}_i = \frac{1}{\Delta} e^{-\beta (E_i + P V_i)},\)\( where \)\Delta = \sum_j e^{-\beta (E_j + P V_j)}\( is the canonical partition function for the isobaric ensemble (\)T,P$ fixed).

Then $\(\begin{align*} S &= -k_B \sum_i \mathbb{P}_i \ln \mathbb{P}_i \\ &= -k_B \sum_i \mathbb{P}_i \Big[-\beta(E_i + P V_i) - \ln \Delta \Big] \\ &= k_B \beta \sum_i \mathbb{P}_i (E_i + P V_i) + k_B \ln \Delta \\ &= k_B \beta \langle E + P V \rangle + k_B \ln \Delta, \end{align*}\)\( where \)\langle \cdot \rangle$ denotes the ensemble average.

Since \(\beta = 1/(k_B T)\), we can write $\(S = \frac{\langle E + P V \rangle}{T} + k_B \ln \Delta.\)$

Up to this point, it is identical to previous page. Now define the enthalpy \(H\) as $\(H \equiv \langle E + P V \rangle.\)$

Then we can express \(H\) in terms of the partition function: $\(H = T S - k_B T \ln \Delta.\)$

Last term on the RHS is Gibbs free energy, so $\(H - T S = G=E - TS+PV\)\( Hence, \)\(H =E +PV\)\( and \)\(dH =-PdV+TdS +PdV+Vdp=TdS+VdP\)$

Key points:#

  • This mirrors the derivation of Gibbs free energy \(G = U - TS + PV = - k_B T \ln \Delta\).

  • Enthalpy naturally arises as the ensemble average of energy plus \(PV\) for the isobaric ensemble.

  • Differential form: $\(dH = T\, dS + V\, dP,\)\( so the natural variables of \)H\( are \)S\( and \)P\(, just as the natural variables of \)G\( are \)T\( and \)P$.

Summary:#

\[\boxed{H = U + PV = \langle E + P V \rangle}, \quad dH = T\,dS + V\,dP.\]

Enthalpy is the thermodynamic potential that is most useful for processes at constant pressure, just as Gibbs free energy is most useful for processes at constant temperature and pressure.\

\(\star\) For ideal gas,
$\(\begin{array}{ccc} PV = nRT &\Rightarrow& H = U + nRT\\ & & \Downarrow\\ & & \underset{C_P}{\underset{\downarrow}{\frac{dH}{dT}}} = \underset{C_V}{\underset{\downarrow}{\frac{dU}{dT}}} + nR \end{array}\)$

Legendre Transforms and the Helmholtz Free Energy {#legendre-transforms-and-the-helmholtz-free-energy .unnumbered}#

Free energy represents the portion of a system’s internal energy that is available to perform work. In the case of the Helmholtz free energy, this quantity corresponds to the energy available to do (typically non-\(PV\)) work under conditions of constant temperature and constant volume.

The Helmholtz free energy is defined as $\(A = U - TS,\)\( which is obtained by subtracting the term \)TS\( from the internal energy \)U\(. Because \)U\( and \)TS\( are both path-independent state functions, their combination is also a state function. The appearance of the negative sign in the definition of \)A$ follows from the physics of thermodynamic consistency.

This construction is an example of a Legendre transform, introduced in an earlier chapter. In thermodynamics, the Legendre transform of a function \(f(x)\) is typically written as $\(\tilde{f}(p) = f(x) - px,\)\( where \)(x, p)\( is a pair of conjugate variables. The extra negative sign in the Legendre transform has physical meaning: it replaces one natural variable with its conjugate while preserving the thermodynamic structure. In doing so, it subtracts the entropy--heat term \)TS\( from the internal energy, leaving the Helmholtz free energy. This quantity represents the portion of the system's energy that is actually available to perform useful work at constant temperature.\ [Important point]{.underline}: We use Legendre transform because we want to exchange variable(difficult to manage experimentally) to more usable one for each experiment.\ To perform Legendre transform, convexity of the function w.r.t. variable x has to be satisfied. This is a great exercise so make sure to finish the HW at the end of this section.\ [Another important point]{.underline}: As shown in previous section, we use the same partition function \)Z$as canonical ensemble (review partition function for NVT). However, for NPT ensemble, the partition function is different as described before(review partition function for NPT). Using correct partition function is critical for computational chemist.\

About conjugate pair {#about-conjugate-pair .unnumbered}#

The products \(TS\) and \(PV\) are special because they are conjugate pairs: $\(T \leftrightarrow S, \qquad P \leftrightarrow V.\)\( Each pair consists of an intensive variable (\)T\( or \)P\() multiplied by its extensive counterpart (\)S\( or \)V\(), and their products have units of energy. This allows us to use them to transform one energy representation into another. For example, applying a Legendre transform to replace the natural variable \)S\( in \)U(S,V)\( yields the Helmholtz free energy \)A(T,V)$.

It is essential that both \(TS\) and \(PV\) are path-independent state functions. Therefore, adding or subtracting these quantities to construct new thermodynamic potentials always produces valid state functions. Each potential simply uses a different set of natural variables chosen to match a particular experimental setup.

Finally, while we have not yet provided the general proof that the internal energy \(U\) is a state function, a concise proof is given in the following page after homework.

Pressure from Helmholtz Free Energy#

In this page, we prove some equation that is used in the Pressure derivation. Let us consider the isentropic process where the entropy is constant through out the process. This means process is adiabatic as well as reversible. In that case,

The Helmholtz free energy is defined as $\(A(T,V) = E - TS.\)$

Its differential is $\(dA = dE - TdS - SdT.\)$

For a closed system (no particle exchange), the first law gives $\(dE = TdS - PdV.\)$

Substituting into the differential of \(A\): $\(dA = (TdS - PdV) - TdS - SdT = -PdV - SdT.\)$

Since \(A = A(T,V)\), we can also write the differential explicitly in terms of partial derivatives: $\(dA = \left(\frac{\partial A}{\partial V}\right)_T dV + \left(\frac{\partial A}{\partial T}\right)_V dT.\)$

Comparing the two expressions for \(dA\), we immediately identify $\(\left(\frac{\partial A}{\partial V}\right)_T = -P, \quad \left(\frac{\partial A}{\partial T}\right)_V = -S.\)$

Thus, the pressure is given by $\(\boxed{P = -\left(\frac{\partial A}{\partial V}\right)_T}.\)$

Optional: Statistical Mechanics Connection.

In the canonical ensemble, \(A\) is related to the partition function \(Z\) via $\(A = -k_B T \ln Z,\)\( so that \)\(P = -\left(\frac{\partial A}{\partial V}\right)_T = k_B T \left(\frac{\partial \ln Z}{\partial V}\right)_T.\)$

Entropy is a State Function#

In this section, we demonstrate that entropy \(S\) is a state function by showing that the differential $\(dS = \frac{1}{T}\left( dU + P\,dV \right)\)\( is *exact*. We will use the canonical ensemble, where the partition function \)Z(T,V)\( is defined as \)\(Z(T,V) = \sum_j e^{-\beta E_j(V)}, \qquad \beta = \frac{1}{k_B T}.\)$

Step 1: Express thermodynamic quantities in terms of \(Z\) {#step-1-express-thermodynamic-quantities-in-terms-of-z .unnumbered}#

From statistical mechanics we know: $\(P = k_B T \left( \frac{\partial \ln Z}{\partial V} \right)_{T}, \qquad U = - \frac{\partial \ln Z}{\partial \beta}.\)$

The heat term at constant volume is $\(\delta q_{\text{rev}} = dU + P\,dV.\)\( Thus the entropy differential is \)\(dS = \frac{1}{T}\left( dU + P\,dV \right).\)$

Write it in terms of \(T\) and \(V\): $\(dS = M(T,V)\,dT + N(T,V)\,dV,\)\( where \)\(M = \frac{1}{T}\left( \frac{\partial U}{\partial T} \right)_V, \qquad N = \frac{1}{T}\left( \frac{\partial U}{\partial V} \right)_T + \frac{P}{T}.\)$

To show \(S\) is a state function, it is enough to show the mixed partials match: $\(\frac{\partial M}{\partial V} = \frac{\partial N}{\partial T}.\)$

Step 2: Compute \(N(T,V)\) explicitly {#step-2-compute-ntv-explicitly .unnumbered}#

Use \(U = -\partial_\beta \ln Z\): $\(\left( \frac{\partial U}{\partial V} \right)_T = -\frac{\partial}{\partial V}\frac{\partial\ln Z}{\partial\beta} = -\frac{\partial^2 \ln Z}{\partial V \partial \beta}.\)$

Therefore, $$N(T,V) = \frac{1}{T}\left( -\frac{\partial^2 \ln Z}{\partial V \partial \beta} \right)

  • \frac{1}{T}\left( k_B T \frac{\partial \ln Z}{\partial V} \right) = -\frac{1}{T}\frac{\partial^2 \ln Z}{\partial V \partial \beta}

  • k_B\frac{\partial \ln Z}{\partial V}.$$

Step 3: Differentiate \(N(T,V)\) with respect to \(T\) {#step-3-differentiate-ntv-with-respect-to-t .unnumbered}#

Here we provide full detail, including the part that “conveniently disappears.’’

\[N(T,V)=\frac{1}{T}\Big(-\frac{\partial^2\ln Z}{\partial V\partial\beta}\Big) + k_B\frac{\partial\ln Z}{\partial V}.\]

Differentiate each part.

(a) Derivative of the first term using the product rule:#

\[\frac{\partial}{\partial T} \left[ \frac{1}{T}\left( -\frac{\partial^2\ln Z}{\partial V\partial\beta} \right) \right] = -\frac{1}{T^2}\Big(-\frac{\partial^2\ln Z}{\partial V\partial\beta}\Big) +\frac{1}{T}\left(-\frac{\partial}{\partial T}\frac{\partial^2\ln Z}{\partial V\partial\beta}\right).\]

Use $\(\frac{\partial}{\partial T} = -\frac{1}{k_B T^2}\frac{\partial}{\partial \beta}.\)$

Thus $$\frac{1}{T}\left(-\frac{\partial}{\partial T}\frac{\partial^2\ln Z}{\partial V\partial\beta}\right)#

\frac{1}{k_B T^3}\frac{\partial^3\ln Z}{\partial V\partial\beta^2}.$$

The full contribution becomes $\(\frac{1}{T^2}\frac{\partial^2\ln Z}{\partial V\partial\beta} + \frac{1}{k_B T^3}\frac{\partial^3\ln Z}{\partial V\partial\beta^2}.\)$

(b) Derivative of the second term:#

\[\frac{\partial}{\partial T}\left[k_B\frac{\partial\ln Z}{\partial V}\right] = k_B\frac{\partial^2\ln Z}{\partial V\partial T} = k_B\left(-\frac{1}{k_B T^2}\right) \frac{\partial^2\ln Z}{\partial V\partial\beta} = -\frac{1}{T^2}\frac{\partial^2\ln Z}{\partial V\partial\beta}.\]

(c) Cancellation#

When we add results from (a) and (b), the \(1/T^2\) terms cancel: $$\frac{\partial N}{\partial T}#

\frac{1}{k_B T^3} \frac{\partial^3\ln Z}{\partial V\partial\beta^2}.$$

Step 4: Compute \(\partial M / \partial V\) and compare {#step-4-compute-partial-m-partial-v-and-compare .unnumbered}#

Similarly, $\(M = \frac{1}{T}\left( \frac{\partial U}{\partial T} \right)_V = \frac{1}{T}\left( -\frac{\partial}{\partial T}\frac{\partial\ln Z}{\partial\beta}\right).\)$

Using \(\partial/\partial T = -\frac{1}{k_B T^2}\partial_\beta\), $\(M=\frac{1}{k_B T^3}\frac{\partial^2\ln Z}{\partial\beta^2}.\)$

Now differentiate with respect to \(V\): $$\frac{\partial M}{\partial V}#

\frac{1}{k_B T^3} \frac{\partial^3\ln Z}{\partial V\partial\beta^2}.$$

This matches exactly the expression above: $$\frac{\partial M}{\partial V}#

\frac{\partial N}{\partial T}.$$

Conclusion {#conclusion .unnumbered}#

Since $\(\frac{\partial M}{\partial V} = \frac{\partial N}{\partial T},\)\( the differential \)dS = M,dT + N,dV\( is *exact*. Therefore, entropy \)S$ is a state function.

Ideal gas law#

Let us think about the meaning of partition function. When you are taking average value of \(x\), you are putting \(x\) in front of \(p(x)\). More precisely, \(x \times events\) divide by the total events. What if you put 1 in front of \(p(x)\) instead? You are simply counting the number of possible outcome. For example, average of exam score is obtained by the number of students who got same score multiplied by the score. Instead, if you replace the score by unity, you are just counting the number of student in the class which eventually serves as a normalization constant. It is really not too obvious from Boltzmann distribution, but we are counting the number of states having \(\epsilon_i\) and summing over all possible i. In similar philosophy, the partition function in classical mechanics is defined over all possible value of position and momenta which satisfy the energy term \(H(p,q)\). \(h^{3}\) is a volume occupied by each state (see previous chapter or appendix). But this goes away when you integrate w.r.t. q and p.
$\(\begin{align*} Z &= \sum\limits_i e^{-\epsilon_i/k_BT}\\ &=\frac{1}{h^{3N}N!} \int\int dq^{3N}dp^{3N} e^{-\left(\frac{p_1^2}{2m}+\frac{p_2^2}{2m} + \cdots + \frac{p_{3N}^2}{2m}\right)/k_BT}\\ &= \frac{V^N}{N!}\underbrace{\left(\int e^\frac{-p^2}{2m/k_BT} dp\right)^{3N}}_{\text{This term does not depend on volume. Call this } f(\beta).} \end{align*}\)\( We pretended that we did actual integration in the above equation so \)h^{3N}\( term disappear from the expression.\ \)\(\begin{align*} \ln Z &= N\ln V + \ln f(\beta) - \ln N! \end{align*}\)$

\[\frac{\partial \ln Z}{\partial V}\Big|_T = \frac{N}{V}\]
\[P= \beta^{-1} \frac{\partial \ln Z}{\partial V}\Big|_T = k_B T\frac{N}{V}\]

There we have

\[PV = nk_BT \hspace{10pt} \leftarrow \text{ ideal gas eq}\]
