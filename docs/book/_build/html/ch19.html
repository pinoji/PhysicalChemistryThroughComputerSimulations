The Equipartition Principle#

Classical#

“Each independent pure quadratic momentum or position term in the classical energy of a particle contribute \(\frac{RT}{2}\) to the thermodynamic internal energy per mole.”

Consider diatomic molecule having harmonic oscillator,

\[KE = \frac{p^2}{2\mu} \hspace{10pt} V = \frac{k_0r^2}{2}\]

Using integral expression (single degree of freedom),

\[\begin{split}\begin{align*} Z &= \frac{1}{h}\int_{-\infty}^{\infty}\int_{-\infty}^{\infty}\exp\left[-\frac{p^2}{2\mu k_BT}-\frac{k_0r^2}{2k_BT}\right]dpdr\\ &\downarrow \text{ using table}\\ &= \frac{1}{h}\sqrt{2\pi \mu k_BT}\cdot\sqrt{\frac{2\pi}{k_0}k_BT}\\ &\text{\hspace{40pt}} \uparrow \text{\hspace{40pt}} \uparrow\\ &\text{\hspace{10pt} \begin{tabular}{c}momentum\\ part\end{tabular} \begin{tabular}{c}position\\ part\end{tabular}} \end{align*}\end{split}\]

Please see appendix for the origin of Plank constant if you are interested.Note that use of integration is justifed because classical treatment allows continous energy in any modes . In general (either classical or quantum),
$\(\ln Z = \frac{1}{2}\ln T + \text{ const }\)$

for pure quadratic term \(p^2\) & \(r^2\).

\[\frac{u}{k_BT} = T\left(\frac{\partial \ln Z}{\partial T}\right)_V = \frac{1}{2}\]
\[u= \frac{1}{2}k_BT \text{ for such term }\]

[HW]{.underline}
Using \(KE=\frac {p^2}{2m}\) and \(V=0\), obtain \(Z_{\text{tran}}\) in classical treatment. Then, obtain \(<E_{\text{tran}}>\)
[HW]{.underline}
Using \(KE=\frac {L^2}{2I}\) and \(V=0\), obtain \(Z_{\text{rot}}\) in classical treatment. Note that angular velocity range contiously from 0 to infinity and so does \(L=I\omega\). Then, obtain \(<E_{\text{rot}}>\) For diatomic molecule, there are two rotations (about x and about y). So total \(<E_r>\) must be twice that of your result. For nonlinear molecule, \(<E_r>\) must be equal to triple that of your result.
[HW]{.underline}
Using \(Z_{\text{vib}}\) in classical treatment shown above, obtain \(<E_{\text{vib}}>\)\

Therefore, at thermodynamic equilibrium, any quadratic

term contributes \(\frac{k_BT}{2}\) to the internal energy.
For a [simple diatomic molecule]{.underline}, the classical equipartition theorem predicts\

Quantum#

Computation of Partition Function \(Z\)
from Physical Chemistry Part I
E\(_\text{tot}\) = Elec + Vib + Rot + Trans\

$\begin{array}{rl} $\boxed{\begin{array}{l} \text{For 2 particle system,}\\ \sum\limits_{rs} e^{-(\underset{\uparrow}{\epsilon_r}+\underset{\uparrow}{\epsilon_s})/k_BT}\\ \text{Energy of particle 1 \hspace{5pt} particle 2}\\ =\left(\sum\limits_r e^{-\epsilon_r/k_BT}\right)\left(\sum\limits_se^{-\epsilon_s/k_BT}\right)\end{array}}$ Z &= \sum\limits_i e^{-E_i/k_BT}\\ & \text{\hspace{10pt}} \downarrow N \text{ particles} \\ &= \frac{1}{N!}\left(\sum\limits_i e^{-\epsilon_i/k_BT}\right)^N\\ %& \text{partition function for each particle}\\ \end{array}$

\(=\frac{1}{N!}\left[\sum\limits_{ijmn}e^{-(Vib_i + Rot_j + Trans_m + Elec_n)/k_BT}\right]^N\)

\(=\frac{1}{N!}\Big[\underbrace{\sum\limits_i e^{-Vib_i/k_BT}}_{q_\text{vib}}\underbrace{\sum\limits_j e^{-Rot_i/k_BT}}_{q_\text{rot}}\underbrace{\sum\limits_m e^{-Trans_m/k_BT}}_{q_\text{trans}}\underbrace{\sum\limits_n e^{-Elec_n/k_BT}}_{q_\text{elec}}\Big]^N\)

\(=\frac{1}{N!}[q_\text{vib } q_\text{rot } q_\text{trans }q_\text{elec}]^N\)

\(\star\) \(q_\text{trans}\)
from Physical Chemistry Part I,
$\(\epsilon_\text{trans} = \frac{h^2}{8ma^2}(n_x^2+n_y^2+n_z^2)\)$

\[\begin{split}\begin{align*} q_\text{trans} &= \sum\limits_{xyz} e^{-\epsilon_{xyz}/k_BT}\\ &=\left[\sum\limits_n e^{-\epsilon_n/k_BT}\right]^3\\ &=\left[\sum\limits_ne^{-\frac{h^2}{8ma^2}n^2/k_BT}\right]^3\\ & \approx \left[ \int_{0}^{\infty} e^{-\frac{h^2}{8ma^2}n^2/k_BT} dn \right]^3\\ & \hspace{20pt} \downarrow \text{ using integration table }\\ &= \left(\frac{2\pi mk_BT}{h^2}\right)^{3/2}a^3\\ &= \left(\frac{2\pi mk_BT}{h^2}\right)^{3/2}V \end{align*}\end{split}\]

The usage of integration is justified by the small step size for the translational energy as explained in Part I of this textbook.

\(\star\) \(q_\text{rot}\)
from Physical Chemistry Part I,
$\(\epsilon_J = \frac{\hbar^2J(J+1)}{2I}\)$

and each energy level has

a degeneracy of \(g_J = 2J + 1\).
$\(q_\text{rot} =\sum\limits_J(2J+1)e^{-\frac{\hbar^2}{2I}\frac{J(J+1)}{k_BT}}\)$

\[\Theta := \frac{\hbar^2}{2Ik_B} = \frac{hB}{k_B} \leftarrow \text{ rotational temperature}\]
\[B = \frac{h}{8\pi^2 I}\]
\[q_{rot} = \sum\limits_J(2J+1) e^{-\Theta_{\text{rot}}J(J+1)/T}\]

For linear molecule,

$\begin{array}{ll} q_{\text{rot}} &= \sum\limits_J (2J+1)e^{-\frac{\Theta}{T}J(J+1)}\\ $\begin{array}{c} & \text{Let } J(J+1) = X.\\ \Theta_r = \frac{h^2}{8\pi^2Ik_B}\\ &= \int e^{-\frac{\Theta}{T}X} dX\\ \uparrow\\ &= \left(\frac{T}{\Theta}\right)e^{-\frac{\Theta}{T}X}\Big|_0^\infty\\ \text{moment of inertia}\\ &= \frac{T}{\Theta} \sum\limits_imr_i^2\\ \end{array}$ \text{ For diatom, }\\ \mu r^2\\ \uparrow\\ \text{ reduced mass}\\ \frac{m_1m_2}{m_1+m_2} \end{array}$

Adjusting to the case A-A or A-B,

\[q_{\text{rot}} = \frac{1}{\sigma_\text{r}}\left(\frac{T}{\Theta_r}\right)\]
\[q_r = \frac{\pi^{1/2}}{\sigma_r}\left(\frac{T^{\frac{3}{2}}}{(\Theta_{rx}\Theta_{ry}\Theta_{rz})^{1/2}}\right)\]

The usage of integration is justified by the small step size for the rotational energy as explained in Part I of this textbook.
[HW]{.underline}
Use this \(q_\text{rot}\) to obtain \(<E_r>\) Does your result agree well with classical version of equipartition result?
\(\star\) \(q_\text{vib}\)
from Physical Chemistry Part I, \(\epsilon_\text{vib} = \left(\nu + \frac{1}{2}\right) \hbar\omega\)

\[\begin{split}\begin{align*} q_\text{vib} &= \sum\limits_\nu e^{-\epsilon_\text{vib}/k_BT}\\ &=\sum\limits_\nu e^{-\left(\nu +\frac{1}{2}\right)\hbar\omega/k_BT}\\ &=e^{-\frac{\hbar\omega}{2k_BT}}\underbrace{\sum\limits_\nu e^{-\nu \hbar\omega/k_BT}}_{\text{geometric series}}\\ &=e^{-\frac{\hbar\omega}{2k_BT}}\frac{1}{1-e^{-\hbar\omega/k_BT}} \end{align*}\end{split}\]

Let \(\Theta_\text{vib}:=\frac{\hbar\omega}{k_B}\) be vibrational temperature. Then,

\[q_\text{vib} = \frac{e^{-\Theta_\text{vib}/2T}}{1-e^{-\Theta_\text{vib}/T}}\]

Here, we used geometric series instead of integration because of the large step size for the vibrational energy as explained in Part I of this textbook.
[HW]{.underline}
Use this \(q_\text{vib}\) to obtain \(<E_v>=\frac{k_B\Theta_\text{vib}}{2}+\frac{k_B\Theta_\text{vib}}{{e^{\Theta_\text{vib}/T}}-1}\).
[HW]{.underline}
Above expression is somewhat complicated to compare with classical result. So we take another derivative with respect to T to obtain \(C_V\) expression \(\frac{k_B\left(\frac{\Theta_\text{vib}}{T}\right)^2e^{\Theta_\text{vib}/T}}{\left( e^{\Theta_\text{vib}/T}-1 \right)^2}\)
Letting \(X=\Theta_\text{vib}/T\), this expression becomes
\(\frac{k_BX^2e^{X}}{\left( e^{X}-1 \right)^2}\)
Now make T large (\(\underset{X\rightarrow 0}{\lim}\)). Since both denominator and numerator approach zero, you may need to use H’lopital rule as many times as you need to obtain the correct limit value of \(k_B\). Show your work.
For polyatomic system,

\[q_\text{vib} = \underset{k}{\Pi}\frac{1}{1-e^{-\Theta_{\nu_k}/T}}\]
\[\uparrow\]

Note I set zero to be at zero point energy(ZPE).

From Physical Chemistry part I, we have learned that the gap between electronic state is huge.

Prove \(dU\) is exact for ideal gas. (S and V) case#

\(du = TdS - PdV\)

\[S = \underset{\uparrow}{\frac{E}{T}} + k_B\ln Z \text{ where } \ln Z = \ln\frac{\left[\left(\frac{2\pi m}{h^2}k_BT\right)^{3/2}V\right]^N}{N!}\]

\(\frac{3}{2}R \leftarrow\) const

\[\begin{split}\begin{align*} k_B\ln Z &= k_B N\frac{3}{2}\ln k_BT + k_BN\ln V + k_BN \ln \frac{2\pi m}{h^2}- k_B\ln N! \cdots \text{\circled{1}} \\ (PV=nRT \rightarrow) &= k_BN\frac{3}{2}\ln \left(\frac{PV}{n}\right) + k_B N\ln \underset{\uparrow const}{V} + \text{ const } \end{align*}\end{split}\]
\[\left(\frac{\partial k_B\ln Z}{\partial P}\right)_V = \frac{3}{2}Nk_B\left(\frac{n}{PV}\right)\frac{V}{n} = \frac{3}{2}Nk_B\frac{1}{P}\]
\[\left(\frac{\partial S}{\partial P}\right)_V = \frac{3}{2}\frac{Nk_B}{P} \Rightarrow \left(\frac{\partial(-P)}{\partial S}\right)_V = -\frac{3}{2}\frac{P}{nk_B}\]

Now we need \(\left(\frac{\partial T}{\partial V}\right)_S = \left(\frac{\partial (-P)}{\partial S}\right)_V = -\frac{3}{2}\frac{P}{nk_B}\).
Using ,\

\(\begin{array}{rl}\left(\frac{\partial T}{\partial V}\right)_S &= -\frac{2}{3}\frac{T}{V} \hspace{10pt}(\text{using } PV = nk_BT)\\ &= -\frac{2}{3}\frac{P}{nk_B}// \end{array}\)
