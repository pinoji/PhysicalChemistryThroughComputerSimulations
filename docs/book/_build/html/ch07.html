System Containing Two Atoms (Diatomic System)#

We now consider the simplest system that containing more than one nuclei. \(H_2^+\) is the simplest system of the diatomic system because there is no electron-electron repulsion. Because the electron is equally distributed, molecular orbital for this atom must be composed of atomic orbitals centered at each of these hydrogen atoms. Unlike single atomic system, atomic orbitals centered at different atoms are not orthogonal. In this chapter, we introduce overlap integral, Coulomb integral, and exchange integral. All of these are fundamental for understanding chemical bonds. Also, term symbols are introduced for diatomic system.

\(H_2^+\) System#

We start with trial function.
\(\psi_{\pm} = C_11S_A \pm C_21S_B\)\

Let \(C_1 = C_2 = 1\) for now. ( normalization is automatically implemented in variational principle.)
\(\hat{H}\psi_+(r) = E_+\psi_+(r)\)
\(E_+ = \frac{\int \psi^\ast\hat{H}\psi dr_1}{\int\psi^\ast\psi dr_1} \rightarrow dr_1\) means coordinate associated with \(e^-\) #1.

Let \(dr_1 = dr\) Lasy is good. Let’s compute the denominator.

\[\begin{split}\begin{array}{ll} \int\psi^\ast\psi dr &= \int(1S_A + 1S_B)^\ast(1S_A + 1S_B) dr\\ &= \underbrace{\int 1S_A^\ast1S_A dr}_{=1} + 2\underbrace{\int1S_A1S_B dr}_{S(R)} + \underbrace{\int1S_B1S_B dr}_{=1}\\ & = 2 + 2S(R) \cdots \text{\textcircled{1}} \end{array}\end{split}\]

Notice \(S(R)\) becomes small as \(R\) gets large.

[i.e.]{.underline}
\(S(R) = e^{-R(1+R+\frac{R^2}{3})}\)

\(\uparrow\)

fitting data

\[\begin{split}\begin{equation*} \begin{split} \int\psi^\ast\hat{H}\psi dr &= \int\left(1S_A + 1S_B\right)^\ast\left[-\frac{1}{2}\nabla^2 - \frac{1}{r_{1A}} - \frac{1}{r_{1B}}\right]\left(1S_A + 1S_B\right) dr\\ &= \int\left(1S_A + 1S_B\right)^\ast\Big[ \hspace{90pt}\Big]1S_A dr \\ & + \int\big(\hspace{50pt}\big)^\ast\Big[\hspace{90pt}\Big]1S_B dr \end{split} \end{equation*}\end{split}\]

Note

\[\begin{split}\begin{equation*} \begin{split} \left[-\frac{1}{2}\nabla^2 - \frac{1}{r_{1A}}\right]1S_A &= E_{1S_A}1S_A \hspace{10pt}\text{same for $E_{1S_B}1S_B$ \& $E_{1S_A} = E_{1S_B}=E_{1S}$}\\ &= \int\left(1S_A + 1S_B\right)^\ast\left[E_{1S} - \frac{1}{r_B}\right]1S_A dr\\ &+ \int\big(\hspace{48pt}\big)^\ast\left[E_{1S} - \frac{1}{r_A}\right]1S_B dr\\ &= \int 1S_A\left[E_{1S} - \frac{1}{r_B}\right]1S_A dr + \int 1S_B\left[E_{1S} - \frac{1}{r_B}\right]1S_A dr\\ & + \int 1S_A\left[E_{1S} - \frac{1}{r_A}\right]1S_B dr + \int 1S_B\left[E_{1S} - \frac{1}{r_A}\right]1S_B dr\\ &=E_{1S}\int 1S_A1S_A dr - \int 1S_A\frac{1}{r_B}1S_A dr + E_{1S}\int 1S_B1S_A dr - \int 1S_B\frac{1}{r_B}1S_A dr + \text{e.t.c.} \end{split} \end{equation*}\end{split}\]

\(\cdots\) same stuff for \(1S_B\)

\[\begin{split}\begin{equation*} \begin{split} &= E_{1S} + \int \underline{\underline{1S_A}}\frac{-1}{r_B}\underline{\underline{1S_A}} dr \rightarrow \text{same orbital call this Coulomb (J)}\\ &+ E_{1S}S(R) + \int\underline{1S_B}\frac{-1}{r_B}\underline{\underline{1S_A}} dr \rightarrow \text{different orbital call this exchange (K)}\\ &+ E_{1S} + \int 1S_A\frac{-1}{r_A}1S_B dr\\ &+ E_{1S}S(R) +\int1S_B\frac{-1}{r_A}1S_Bdr\\ &= E_{1S}\left(2 + 2S(R)\right) + 2J + 2K \cdots \text{\textcircled{2}} \end{split} \end{equation*}\end{split}\]

\(\xRightarrow{\text{\textcircled{1} \& \textcircled{2}}}\) So, Energya of \(H_2^+\) for \(\psi_+\) is

\[\begin{split}\begin{equation*} \begin{split} E_+ = \frac{\int\psi_+^\ast H \psi_+ dr}{\int\psi_+^\ast\psi_+dr} &= \frac{2E_{1S}\left(1 + S(R)\right) + 2J + 2K}{2 + 2S(R)}\\ &=E_{1S} + \frac{J + K}{1 + S(R)} \end{split} \end{equation*}\end{split}\]

relative \(E_+\) compares to \(E_{1S}\)

\[\begin{equation*} \Delta E = E_+ - E_{1S} = \frac{J + K}{1 + S(R)} \end{equation*}\]

We can find the same result if we use secular determinant.
$\(\begin{vmatrix} H_{AA} - ES_{AA} & H_{AB} - ES_{AB}\\ H_{BA} - ES_{BA} & H_{BB} - ES_{BB}\end{vmatrix} = 0\)$

This is an alternative way, but I want you to …
[HW]{.underline} derive

\[\begin{equation*} \Delta E = E_- - E_{1S} = \frac{J - K}{1 - S} \end{equation*}\]

Molecular Term Symbol for Diatom#

It follows similar procedure as in atom.
$\(\begin{array}{lccl} \text{atom ind. orb AM} & l & \rightarrow & \begin{array}{cccc} s & p & d & f\\ 0 & 1 & 2 & 3 \end{array}\\ \text{atom total orb AM} & L & \rightarrow & \begin{array}{cccc} S & P & D & F\\ 0 & 1 & 2 & 3 \end{array}\\ \text{diatom ind. orb AM} & \lambda & \rightarrow & \begin{array}{cccc} \sigma & \pi & \delta & \phi\\ 0 & 1 & 2 & 3 \end{array}\\ \text{diatom total orb AM} & \Lambda & \rightarrow & \begin{array}{cccc} \Sigma & \Pi & \Delta & \Phi\\ 0 & 1 & 2 & 3 \end{array} \end{array}\)$

\[\begin{split}\begin{array}{l} \begin{array}{rl} $atom for given $ L & -L \le M_L \le L\\ $\underline{i.e.}\hspace{5pt}$ L = 2 & M_L = -2, -1, 0, 1, 2 \end{array} \\ \\ \begin{array}{rl} $diatom for given $ \Lambda & M_\Lambda = \pm \Lambda\\ $\underline{i.e.}\hspace{5pt}$ \Lambda = 2 & M_\Lambda = \pm 2 $ only$ \end{array} \end{array}\end{split}\]

For example, \(p_z\) orbital has \(m_{\lambda}\) value of 0 while \(p_x\) and \(p_y\) has 1 and -1, respectively.
\(S\) is identical to atomic case.
