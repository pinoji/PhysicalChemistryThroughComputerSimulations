Simple Quantum Mechanical Models and Solutions#

\(\hspace{30pt}\)Now that we have reviewed postulates of quantum mechanics, it is time for us to apply them to some simple systems. By doing so, we may find some interesting outcomes that arise from quantum mechanics. Before we dive into quantum mechanics, we shall cover few physical concepts that are vital to physical chemists.

When Is Quantum Mechanics Important?#

$\hspace{30pt}\(Quantum mechanics is certainly important for the electronic property of the molecules as Bohr's model incorporate wave nature of electron through de Broglie relation. Since wave-nature of an electron has been demonstrated by double slit experiment, interest readers may look for an online video. For this reason, it is appropriate to apply Schrodinger's formulation which shares the same foundation to wave mechanics. A considerable amount of part 1 of this textbook deal with the quantum state of the electron. However, solving electronic state is just a part of the problem and it is certainly not enough to model any chemical reaction, the ultimate goal for physical chemistry.\ \)\hspace{30pt}\(Because postulates of quantum mechanics is applied to not only apply to electrons as well as neclei, Shrödinger Equation is generally difficult to solve, even for simple atomic problems. Meanwhile, we know that the mass of an electron (0.00055AMU) is significantly smaller than that of any nuclei (\)\geq\( 1AMU). Therefore, an electron moves much faster than any of the nuclei in the molecule. In general, it is safe to assume that the motion of atomic nuclei and electrons in a molecule can be separated. This approximation, called the Born-Oppenheimer approximation, greatly simplifies the problem. After separating the motions of the nucleus and the electrons, one generally solves first the part of the electrons associated with the molecule of interest and then proceeds to the motions of the nucleus. After all, chemical reactions are nothing more than rearrangements of atoms, so to consider chemical reactions from a thermodynamic point of view, one must take into account the vibrations, rotations, and translations of the products and reactants.\ \)\hspace{30pt}$Consider a molecule consist of 3N atoms. Since each atom can move along x,y, & z-direction, the total degrees of freedom is equal to 3N. If all atoms move in the same direction, center of mass moves. Therefore, three degrees of freedom is used for translation of the center of mass. If the molecule is non-linear, the molecule can be rotated around x, y, or z-axis; three degrees of freedom is used for rotational modes. Rest of the degrees of freedom (3N-6) is considered as vibrational modes. If the molecule is linear, the molecule can be rotated around only 2 axes; two degrees of freedom is used for rotational modes. Rest of the degrees of freedom (3N-5) is considered as vibrational modes. As Planck pointed out, energy is quantized. However, the energy gap between quantum state is so small for some of the modes; it can be approximated classically to simplify the problem. We shall cover some of these in this chapter.\

1-D Shrödinger Equation (1-D P.I.B.)#

Shrödinger Equation
\(-\frac{\hbar^2}{2m}\frac{d^2}{dx^2}\psi + V(x) \psi = E\psi\)
\(\hspace{10pt} \downarrow\)

\(\frac{d^2}{dx^2}\psi + q\psi = 0 (0\leq x \leq a)\)
\(q = \frac{2mE}{\hbar^2}\)
Recall our D.E.

\(y" + py' + qy = 0\)

\(\hspace{20pt}\uparrow\)

\(\hspace{20pt} \cancel{0}\)
Guess \(y = e^{sx}\)
\(s^2 e^{sx} + q e^{sx} = 0\)
\(s^2 + q = 0\)
\(s = \pm\sqrt{q} i = \pm \frac{(2mE)^{1/2}}{\hbar} i = \pm ki\)
So our solution is

\(\psi = C_1 e^{ikx} + C_2 e^{-ikx}\)
\(=(C_1+C_2)\cos(kx) + i(C_1-C_2)\sin{kx} \because e^{iax}=\cos(ax)+i\sin(ax)\)
\(=A\cos(kx) + B\sin(kx)\)

Applying the boundary condition,
\(\psi(0) = \psi(a) = 0 \Rightarrow A = 0\)
\(\hspace{90pt} \psi(a) = B\sin(ka) = 0\)
\(\hspace{90pt} ka = n\pi\)
\(\hspace{120pt} \Downarrow\)
So, \(\psi(x) = B\sin(kx) \hspace{20pt} \frac{(2mE)^{1/2}}{\hbar} a = n\pi (n = 0, 1, 2, \cdots)\)
\(\hspace{38pt} = B\sin\Big(\frac{n\pi x}{a}\Big)\)
\(\hspace{120pt} E = \frac{\pi^2 n^2 \hbar^2}{2ma^2}\)
\(\hspace{130pt} \downarrow ( \hbar = \frac{h}{2\pi})\)
\(\hspace{130pt} = \frac{n^2h^2}{8ma^2}\)
We still need to find B.
[requirement]{.underline} \(\int \psi^* \psi dx = 1\)
\(1 = |B|^2\int_0^a\sin^2\big(\frac{n\pi x}{a}\big) dx\)
\(\hspace{10pt} = \frac{a|B|^2}{n\pi}\int_0^{n\pi}\sin^2 z dz \hspace{20pt} z = \frac{n\pi x}{a}\)
\(\hspace{10pt} = |B|^2\frac{a}{n\pi}\big(\frac{n\pi}{2}\big) = |B|^2\frac{a}{2}\)
\(B = \sqrt{\frac{2}{a}}\) So, \(\psi = \sqrt{\frac{2}{a}}\sin\big(\frac{n\pi x}{a}\big) \hspace{10pt} n = 1, 2, 3, \cdots\)

Harmonic Oscillator#

Classical#

In harmonic oscillator, restoring force is given by

$f = -k(l - l_0) = -kx$.
By Newton force balance,\

\(f = ma\)

\(m\frac{d^2x}{dt^2} + kx = 0\)

By same ODE technique,\

\(x = c_1\sin(\omega t) + c_2\cos(\omega t) = A\sin(\omega t + \phi)\)

\(\hspace{60pt}\) where \(\omega = \Big(\frac{k}{m}\Big)^{1/2}\)

From Physics I

\(\hspace{20pt}\hookrightarrow\)

\(f(x) = -\frac{\partial V}{\partial x} \Rightarrow V = \frac{1}{2} kx^2\)

\(H = \frac{p^2}{2m} + V = \frac{m}{2}\big(\frac{dx}{dt}\big)^2 + \frac{1}{2} kx^2 = E\)

If we choose \(x(t) = A\cos(\omega t)\), then,\

\(E = \frac{1}{2}m\omega^2A^2\cos^2(\omega t) + \frac{1}{2} kA^2\sin^2(\omega t)\)

\(\omega = \big(\frac{k}{m}\big)^{1/2} \Rightarrow \hspace{10pt} E = \frac{kA^2}{2}\big(\sin^2(\omega t) + \cos^2(\omega t)\big)\)

\(\hspace{80pt} =\frac{kA^2}{2} \hspace{10pt} \leftarrow\) Energy is constant w.r.t. time.

Center of Mass & Reduced Mass (2-body problem to 1-body)#

Center of mass for 2-body is defined as\

\(X = \frac{m_1x_1 + m_2x_2}{m_1 + m_2} = \frac{m_1x_1 + m_2 x_2}{M}\)

By force(ma) balance(sum of change in momentum of each fragment),\

\(\frac{d^2}{dt^2}\big(MX\big) = \frac{d^2}{dt^2}\big(m_1x_1 + m_2x_2\big) = 0\)

So, \(X\) is stationary.
Newton’s force balance

\[\begin{split}\begin{equation*} \begin{split} m_1\frac{d^2x_1}{dt^2} &= k\big(x_2 - x_1 - l_0\big)\\ m_2\frac{d^2x_2}{dt^2} &= -k\big(x_2 - x_1 - l_0\big) \end{split} \end{equation*}\end{split}\]

Letting \(x= x_2 - x_1 - l_0\),\

\(\frac{d^2x}{dt^2} = \frac{d^2dx_2}{dt^2} - \frac{d^2x_1}{dt^2} = -k\big(\frac{1}{m_1} + \frac{1}{m_2}\big)x =-kx\Big(\frac{m_1 + m_2}{m_1m_2}\Big)\)

alg \(\downarrow\)\

\(\mu\frac{d^2x}{dt^2} + kx = 0\) where \(\mu = \frac{m_1m_2}{m_1 + m_2}\)

\(\downarrow\)

\(\mu\) is the reduced mass.

Quantum Mechanics Version of Harmonic Oscillator#

\(\hat{H}\psi = E\psi\)
\(\hat{H} = \hat{T} + \hat{V}\)
\(-\frac{\hbar^2}{2\mu}\frac{d^2\psi}{dx^2} + V(x)\psi = E\psi\)
\(\frac{d^2\psi}{dx^2} + \frac{2\mu}{\hbar^2}\Big(E - \frac{1}{2}kx^2\Big)\psi = 0\)
The solution is already found by mathematician.
\(\psi_v(x) = N_v\uwave{H_v\big(\alpha^{1/2}x\big)}e^{-\alpha x^2/2}\)
called Hermite polynomial
\(\alpha = -\big(\frac{k\mu}{\hbar^2}\big)^{1/2}\)
\(N_v = \frac{1}{(2^vv!)^{1/2}}\big(\frac{\alpha}{\pi}\big)^{1/4}\)

More Realistic Potential#

We have learned that Harmonic Oscillator has infinite potential at large bond distance, and the molecule will not break apart no matter how strong they oscillate. This is a very poor approximation for the chemistry and more acculate model should be introduced.

Morse Potential#

Potential energy of Morse potential is given as
\(E_v=D\left( 1-e^{(-\alpha(l-l_o))}\right)^2\)
where \(\alpha=\sqrt{\frac{k_e}{2D_e}}\)
\(D_e\) represent the classical well depth. Note that harmonic oscillator is similar to the Morse potential near the bottom of the well.
[HW]{.underline} Show that Harmonic potential is identical to the Morse potential near the bottom of the potential well. Hint: Expand the exponential
It can be shown that allowed vibrational energy of the morse potential (up to some point) is given as
\(E_v=\hbar v_o(v+\frac{1}{2})-\frac{\left( \hbar v_o(v+\frac{1}{2}) \right)^2}{4D_e}\)
where \(v_o=\alpha\sqrt{\frac{2D_e}{m}}=\sqrt{\frac{k_e}{m}}\)\

Note that energy gap becomes narrower as \(v\) becomes large. This is and harmonic oscillator fails to predict this correct behavior .\

Lenerd Jones Potential#

This potential (also known as 6-12 potential) is a popular choice for modeling chemistry problems because of its simplicity.
\(V_{LJ}=4\epsilon\left[ \left( \frac{\sigma}{r}\right)^{12}-\left( \frac{\sigma}{r}\right)^{6} \right]\)

A Mass Orbiting in Circular Path#

Circular path \(\perp\) to \(x\) at \(\overrightarrow{r} = \overrightarrow{x}\)
$\(\begin{equation*} \begin{split} L_z = \overrightarrow{r} \times \overrightarrow{P} &= xP_y - yP_x \\ &= xP_y \\ & \downarrow |v|\text{ constant magnitude }\\ &= xmv\\ &= rmv\\ &=mr^2\frac{v}{r}\\ &\downarrow \text{ momentum of inertia } I = mr^2\\ &= I\frac{v}{r} = I\omega \end{split} \end{equation*}\)$

Let \(\nu_{rot} =\) frequency of rotation
\(v = 2\pi r \nu_{rot} \Rightarrow \frac{v}{r} = 2\pi \nu_{rot} = \omega \leftarrow\) angular velocity
The equation should look similar to linear momentum.
\(p = m\overrightarrow{v} \hspace{20pt} L_z = I\omega\)
\(\hspace{22pt} \uparrow \hspace{10pt} \nwarrow \hspace{39pt} \uparrow \hspace{9pt} \nwarrow\)
\(\hspace{10pt}\) mass \(\hspace{2pt}\) velocity \(\hspace{2pt}\) inertia \(\hspace{2pt}\) angular velocity

K.E. Associated with Circular Motion#

[Classical Version]{.underline}

\[\begin{split}\begin{equation*} \begin{split} T_{\text{linear}} &= \frac{p^2}{2m} = \frac{1}{2} mv^2\\ T_{\text{ang}} &= \frac{L_z^2}{2I} = \frac{1}{2} I\omega^2 \end{split} \end{equation*}\end{split}\]

[Quantum Mechanics Version]{.underline}


Because \(L_z = \pm pr = \pm \frac{hr}{\lambda}\) (By de Broglie \(p = \frac{h}{\lambda}\))
From boundary condition, $\(\begin{equation*} \begin{split} 2\pi r &= m_l\lambda \hspace{10pt} m_l = 0, \pm1, \pm2, \pm3, \cdots\\ L_z &= \frac{m_lh}{2\pi} = m_l\hbar \end{split} \end{equation*}\)$

So, K.E. of circular motion is
\(E = \frac{L_z^2}{2I} = \frac{(m_l\hbar^2)}{2I}\)


Assume particle follow circular orbit
on \(xy-\) plane (no potential \(V(x) = 0\))

\(\hspace{50pt}\) \(\hspace{20pt}\)
So, \(\hat{H} = -\frac{\hbar^2}{2mr^2}\frac{d^2}{d\phi^2} = -\frac{\hbar^2}{2I}\frac{d^2}{d\phi^2}\)

\(\hspace{88pt} \rightarrow\)

\(\hspace{78pt} I = mr^2\)
Shrödinger Equation is
\(-\frac{\hbar^2}{2I}\frac{d^2}{d\phi^2}\psi = E\psi\)

\(\hspace{40pt} \Big\downarrow\) This is identical to 1-D Shrödinger Equation

\(\frac{d^2}{d\phi^2}\psi + \frac{2IE}{\hbar^2}\psi = 0\)

\(\hspace{40pt} \Big\downarrow m_l^2 = \frac{2IE}{\hbar^2}\)

\(\frac{d^2}{d\phi^2}\psi + m_l^2\psi = 0\)

\(\psi(\phi) = A e^{im_e\phi}\)

\(\hat{L}_z\) Operator and Quantum Number Relation: \(|m| \le l\), with \(m = 0, \pm1, \pm2, \ldots, \pm l\)#

Recall, \(\xrightarrow{\text{Calc III}}\)

\[\begin{split}\begin{equation*} \begin{split} \hat{L}_x = -i\hbar\Big(y\frac{\partial}{\partial z} - z\frac{\partial}{\partial y}\Big) &= -i\hbar\Big(-\sin\phi\frac{\partial}{\partial\theta} - \cot\theta\cos\phi\frac{\partial}{\partial\phi}\Big)\\ \hat{L}_y = -i\hbar\Big(z\frac{\partial}{\partial x} - x\frac{\partial}{\partial z}\Big) &= -i\hbar\Big(\cos\phi\frac{\partial}{\partial\theta} - \cot\theta\sin\phi\frac{\partial}{\partial\phi}\Big)\\ \hat{L}_z = -i\hbar\Big(x\frac{\partial}{\partial y} - y\frac{\partial}{\partial x}\Big) &= -i\hbar\Big(\frac{\partial}{\partial\phi}\Big) \end{split} \end{equation*}\end{split}\]

Only useful one is \(\hat{L}_z\) & I show you part of it.
All \(Y(\theta, \phi)\) has form \(Y(\theta, \phi) = W(\theta)e^{im\phi}\), \(m = \pm0, \pm1, \cdots\).
\(\hat{L}_z\psi = ////\hat{L}_ze^{im\phi} = //// (-i\hbar)\frac{\partial}{\partial\phi}e^{im\phi}\)
\(= //// \hbar me^{im\phi}\)
\(= m\hbar\psi\)
Because we can get the eigenvalues of \(\hat{L}^2\) & \(\hat{L}_z\) from \(\psi\),
they should commute.
then,
\(\hat{L}_z^2\psi = m^2\hbar^2\psi\)
also,
\(\hat{L}^2\psi = l(l + 1)\hbar^2\psi\)
Because
\(\hat{L}^2 = \big(\hat{L}_x^2 + \hat{L}_y^2 + \hat{L}_z^2\big)\)
\(\big(\hat{L}^2 - \hat{L}_z^2\big)\psi = \big(\underbrace{l(l + 1) - m^2}\big)\hbar^2\psi\)
\(\hat{L}_x^2 + \hat{L}_y^2\) They must be both positive.
\(l(l + 1) - m^2 \ge 0\) \(m\) & \(l\) are both integers.
\(|m| \le l \cdots m = 0, \pm1, \pm2, \cdots, \pm l\)
as expected from General Chemistry I.

Dimentionless Eq (atomic unit)#

Recall Shrödinger Equation for H atom.

\[\begin{equation*} \Big[-\frac{\hbar^2}{2m_e}\nabla^2 - \frac{e^2}{4\pi\epsilon_0}\frac{1}{r}\Big]\psi = E\psi \cdots \text{\textcircled{1}} \end{equation*}\]

The coordinate we use for 1⃝ is \((x, y, z)\).

Now, let
$\(\begin{array}{lcl} \left.\begin{array}{l} x = \lambda x'\\ y = \lambda y'\\ z = \lambda z'\end{array}\right) & \Rightarrow & \begin{array}{l} x' = \frac{x}{\lambda}\\y' = \frac{y}{\lambda}\\ z' = \frac{z}{\lambda}\end{array} \end{array}\)$

then, \(\frac{\partial}{\partial x} = \frac{1}{\lambda}\frac{\partial}{\partial x'}\) & \(\frac{\partial^2}{\partial x^2} = \frac{1}{\lambda^2}\frac{\partial^2}{\partial {x'}^2}\) & \(\frac{1}{r} = \frac{1}{\lambda}\frac{1}{r'}\)
1⃝ becomes
$\(\begin{equation*} \left[-\frac{\hbar^2}{2m_e\lambda^2}{\nabla'}^2 - \frac{e^2}{4\pi\epsilon_0\lambda}\frac{1}{r'}\right]\psi(x', y', z') = E\psi(x', y', z') \end{equation*}\)$

We can choose \(\lambda\) such that

\[\begin{split}\begin{array}{cccc} \underbrace{\frac{\hbar^2}{m_e\lambda^2} = \frac{e^2}{4\pi\epsilon_0\lambda}} & \Rightarrow& \lambda = \underbrace{\frac{4\pi\epsilon_0\hbar^2}{m_e e^2}} &= \underbrace{a_0}\\ $dimension is in energy$ & & \hspace{10pt} $unit of distance$ &\hspace{10pt} $Bohr radius$\\ $call this as$ E_a & & & \end{array}\end{split}\]

Plug them into 2⃝
\(E_a\left[-\frac{1}{2}{\nabla'}^2 - \frac{1}{r'}\right] = E\psi\)

\(\Downarrow\)

\[\begin{equation*} \left[-\frac{1}{2}{\nabla'}^2 - \frac{1}{r'}\right]\psi(x', y', z') = \frac{E}{\epsilon_a}(x', y', z') \cdots \text{\textcircled{3}} \end{equation*}\]

Note that \(\frac{E}{\epsilon_a}\) has no dimension.
Neither do \(x', y', z'\) & \(\frac{d}{dx'}\) e.t.c.

\(\frac{x}{\lambda}\)
So, 3⃝ has no dimension at all. //
