#  Boltzmann Distribution 

## Deriving Boltzmann distribution for isolated system

Consider isolated system with $N$ particles (microcanonical ensemble).
Each state is composed of N particle where $N_i$ particles having energy
state $\epsilon_i$, $i = 0, 1, \cdots, r$. This means total energy of
the system is fixed at $E$. We are interested in the property shared in
most number of states in the ensemble.

$$\sum\limits_{i=0}^{r-1} N_i = N$$

$$\sum\limits_{i=0}^{r-1} N_i \epsilon_i = E$$

\# of possible state where $N_i$ occupies $\epsilon_i$th energy state is
given by

$$\Omega = \frac{N!}{N_0!N_1! \cdots N_{r-1}!}$$

Stirling with 1 not omitted(see previous chapter)

$$\begin{align*}
\ln \Omega &= \ln N! - \sum\limits_{0}^{r-1} \ln N_i!\\
& = N\ln N - N + 1 - \sum\limits_0^{r-1} N_i\ln N_i + \sum\limits_0^{r-1} N_i - r\\
& = N\ln N - \sum\limits_0^{r-1} N_i\ln N_i + 1 - r
\end{align*}$$

Set\
$$f = N \underset{\uparrow}{\ln} N - \sum\limits_0^{r-1} N_i\ln N_i + \underset{\uparrow}{1} -\underset{\uparrow}{ r}$$

constant constants\
Since we are interested in the most probable states,\
$$\begin{align*}
\delta f = \delta\left(\sum\limits_0^{r-1} N_i \ln N_i\right) &= \sum\limits_0^{r-1} \delta N_i \ln N_i + \sum\limits_0^{r-1} N_i \delta \ln N_i \\
&= \sum\limits_0^{r-1} \delta N_i \ln N_i + \sum\limits_0^{r-1} \delta Ni
\end{align*}$$ The last equality follows from derivative of natural log.
Also,

$$\begin{align*}
\delta N &= \sum \delta N_i = 0 \\
\delta E &= \sum \epsilon_i \delta N_i = 0
\end{align*}$$

Define

$$\begin{align*}
\delta  \mathscr{L} &= \sum\limits_i \delta N_i + \sum\limits_i \delta N_i \ln N_i + \alpha \sum\limits_i \delta N_i + \beta \sum\limits_i \epsilon_i \delta N_i \\
&= \sum\limits_i (\underbrace{1 + \ln N_i + \alpha + \beta \epsilon_i}_{\underset{=0}{\Downarrow}} ) \delta N_i = 0
\end{align*}$$

$$\ln N_i = -(1 + \alpha + \beta \epsilon_i) => N_i = \underbrace{e^{-(1+\alpha)}}_{\gamma} e^{-\beta\epsilon_i} = \gamma e^{-\beta\epsilon_i}$$

We also know that

$$N = \sum\limits_i N_i = \gamma \sum\limits_i e^{-\beta \epsilon_i}$$

So,

$$\gamma = \frac{N}{\sum\limits_i e^{-\beta\epsilon_i}}$$

and Boltzmannn distribution predicts the probability of having $N_i$
particle having energy $\epsilon_i$\
$$\boxed{\mathbb{P}(X=\epsilon_i) =\mathbb{P}_i= \frac{N_i}{N} = \frac{e^{-\beta\epsilon_i}}{\sum\limits_i e^{-\beta\epsilon_i}}}$$
we use this formula often until we identify the multiplier $\beta$ [^1]\
For the classical system, we will use this probability distribution to
compute for average energy of the system(assume energy is nearly
continuous)\
$<E>=\sum\limits_i\epsilon_i\mathbb{P}_i = \frac{\sum\limits_i\epsilon_ie^{-\beta\epsilon_i}}{\sum\limits_i e^{-\beta\epsilon_i}}\simeq\frac{\int_{0}^{\infty} \epsilon e^{-\beta\epsilon}d\epsilon}{\sum\limits_i e^{-\beta\epsilon_i}}= \frac{1}{\beta}\frac{\sum\limits_ie^{-\beta\epsilon_i}}{\sum\limits_i e^{-\beta\epsilon_i}}=\frac{1}{\beta}$\
where we used integration by parts.\
Note that we will come back to the last formula in the previous page
when we define the entropy.\

## Deriving the partition function for the NVT ensemble (canonical ensemble)

This is the standard statistical--mechanical derivation typically used
in undergraduate physical chemistry.

Consider a system in a particular microstate with energy $E_i$, in
thermal contact with a very large heat bath. The system and bath
together form an isolated composite whose total energy
$E_{\mathrm{tot}}$ is fixed. Because the bath is much larger than the
system, the system can exchange energy with it while remaining at a
constant temperature $T$. (The fact that the kinetic energies of the
system's particles will then obey a thermal distribution need not
concern us in this first pass.)

A key conceptual point: a *microstate* of the system is a single point
in phase space, fully specified by the positions and momenta of all
particles, $\{q_i,p_i\}$. Thus a microstate has no degeneracy: once
$\{q_i,p_i\}$ is fixed, there is exactly one such state.

By contrast, the heat bath has an enormous number of accessible
microstates. If the system has energy $E_i$, then the bath energy is
$E_B = E_{\mathrm{tot}}-E_i$, and the number of bath microstates
consistent with this energy is
$$\Omega_B(E_B) = \Omega_B(E_{\mathrm{tot}}-E_i).$$ Because
probabilities in the microcanonical ensemble are proportional to the
number of accessible microstates, the probability of the system being in
microstate $i$ is proportional to $\Omega_B(E_{\mathrm{tot}} - E_i)$.

Working with logarithms of large quantities is convenient, so we expand
$$\ln\Omega_B(E_{\mathrm{tot}} - E_i)$$ in a Taylor series around
$E_{\mathrm{tot}}$: $$\ln\Omega_B(E_{\mathrm{tot}} - E_i)
   = \ln\Omega_B(E_{\mathrm{tot}})
   - \left.\frac{\partial \ln\Omega_B}{\partial E}\right|_{E_{\mathrm{tot}}} E_i 
   + \cdots .$$

Exponentiating gives $$\Omega_B(E_{\mathrm{tot}} - E_i)
 = \Omega_B(E_{\mathrm{tot}})
   \exp\!\left[
     -\left.
      \frac{\partial \ln\Omega_B}{\partial E}
     \right|_{E_{\mathrm{tot}}}
     E_i
   \right]
   \quad (\text{higher-order terms neglected}).$$

Since $\Omega_B(E_{\mathrm{tot}})$ is a normalization constant
independent of $i$, the probability of the system being in microstate
$i$ is $$\mathbb{P}_i
  = \frac{\Omega_B(E_{\mathrm{tot}} - E_i)}{\Omega_B(E_{\mathrm{tot}})}
  = \exp\!\left[
     -\left.
      \frac{\partial \ln\Omega_B}{\partial E}
     \right|_{E_{\mathrm{tot}}}
     E_i
   \right].$$

We now define the quantity $$\beta \equiv 
\left.
\frac{\partial \ln\Omega_B}{\partial E}
\right|_{E_{\mathrm{tot}}}.$$ Because the bath is macroscopic, $E_i$ is
negligibly small compared with $E_{\mathrm{tot}}$, so the linear
approximation is extremely accurate and $\beta$ may be treated as a
constant. Thus, $$\mathbb{P}_i \propto e^{-\beta E_i}.$$

For students who have completed the later thermodynamics chapters: since
$S_B = k_B \ln \Omega_B$, $$\frac{\partial \ln\Omega_B}{\partial E}
 = \frac{1}{k_B}\frac{\partial S_B}{\partial E}
 = \frac{1}{k_B} \frac{1}{T},$$ where the final equality is the
thermodynamic definition of temperature. Thus, in equilibrium,
$\beta = 1/(k_B T)$ and we recover the familiar Boltzmann factor.\

### Canonical partition function {#canonical-partition-function .unnumbered}

Since every microstate $i$ of the system with energy $E_i$ is equally
valid, the probability of a specific microstate is
$$\mathbb{P}_i \propto e^{-\beta E_i}.$$

To normalize this probability distribution, define the canonical (NVT)
partition function: $$Z(N,V,T) 
= \sum_{i} e^{-\beta E_i}.$$

In classical phase-space form(optional): $$Z(N,V,T)
= \frac{1}{N!h^{3N}}
\int d^{3N}p \int d^{3N}q \;
\exp\!\left[-\beta H(p,q)\right].$$ where unit phase space volume $h^3$
comes from uncertainty principle $\Delta x \Delta p_x \ge h/2$ hence
$\Delta x \Delta p_x = h$ by Ehrenfest and Tolman (1911--1918). The
normalized probability of a microstate is therefore
$$\mathbb{P}_i = \frac{e^{-\beta E_i}}{Z(N,V,T)}.$$

## Deriving the partition function for the NPT ensemble (Most useful ensemble)

Consider a small system (fixed $N$) in contact with a very large
reservoir that can exchange both energy and volume with the system. The
composite (system + reservoir) is isolated with fixed total energy
$E_{\mathrm{tot}}$ and total volume $V_{\mathrm{tot}}$. A microstate of
the system is completely specified by the particle coordinates, momenta,
and the instantaneous volume $V$; let its energy be $E$.

If the system happens to be in a state with energy $E$ and volume $V$,
then the reservoir must have $$E_B = E_{\mathrm{tot}} - E,\qquad 
V_B = V_{\mathrm{tot}} - V.$$ Because the composite is microcanonical,
the probability of this system microstate is proportional to the number
of compatible reservoir microstates,
$$\mathbb{P}(E,V) \;\propto\; \Omega_B(E_{\mathrm{tot}}-E,\; V_{\mathrm{tot}}-V).$$

To proceed, expand the logarithm of the reservoir multiplicity about its
value at $(E_{\mathrm{tot}}, V_{\mathrm{tot}})$: $$\begin{aligned}
\ln\Omega_B(E_{\mathrm{tot}}-E,\;V_{\mathrm{tot}}-V)
&= \ln\Omega_B(E_{\mathrm{tot}},V_{\mathrm{tot}}) \\
&\quad - \left.\frac{\partial \ln\Omega_B}{\partial E}\right|_{\mathrm{tot}}\, E
      - \left.\frac{\partial \ln\Omega_B}{\partial V}\right|_{\mathrm{tot}}\, V
      + \mathcal{O}(E^2,EV,V^2).
\end{aligned}$$ Since the reservoir is macroscopic, the system's
exchanges are tiny by comparison, and the higher-order terms are
negligible.

Exponentiating gives $$\Omega_B(E_{\mathrm{tot}}-E,\;V_{\mathrm{tot}}-V)
\approx \Omega_B(E_{\mathrm{tot}},V_{\mathrm{tot}})
\exp\!\left[
 -\Big(\left.\frac{\partial \ln\Omega_B}{\partial E}\right|_{\mathrm{tot}} E
        +\left.\frac{\partial \ln\Omega_B}{\partial V}\right|_{\mathrm{tot}} V\Big)
\right].$$

Define the coefficients
$$\beta \equiv \left.\frac{\partial \ln\Omega_B}{\partial E}\right|_{\mathrm{tot}},
\qquad
\beta P \equiv \left.\frac{\partial \ln\Omega_B}{\partial V}\right|_{\mathrm{tot}}.$$

**Important note for students:** At this stage, $P$ is simply a
convenient symbol for the coefficient of $V$ when we factor out the same
$\beta$ that appears in the energy term. You do *not* need to know the
thermodynamic definition of pressure yet. This notation becomes
physically meaningful only after reading later chapters.

With this shorthand,
$$\mathbb{P}(E,V) \;\propto\; e^{-\beta(E + P V)}.$$

This leads to the isothermal--isobaric partition function,
$$\Delta(N,P,T)
= \sum_{\text{microstates } i} e^{-\beta(E_i + P V_i)},$$ or in
classical phase-space form (optional), $$\Delta(N,P,T)
= \frac{1}{N! h^{3N}}
\int_0^\infty dV\, e^{-\beta P V}
\int d^{3N}p \int d^{3N}q \; e^{-\beta H(p,q;V)}.$$

**A practical comment for students in biology and chemistry:**

Most experiments in aqueous biological systems effectively take place at
fixed pressure and fixed volume. This is because (i) liquids are nearly
incompressible, so $V$ barely fluctuates, and (ii) the pressure is just
the ambient atmospheric pressure applied by the surrounding reservoir.
Thus the NPT ensemble is often the most appropriate theoretical
description for simulations and for interpreting experimental
observables.

The normalized probability density for a microstate $\rho(p,q,V)$ is
therefore
$$\rho(p,q,V) = \frac{1}{\Delta(N,P,T)} \, e^{-\beta\left(H(p,q;V) + P V\right)}.$$

##  Maxwell Boltzmann Speed Distribution

From Boltzmann dist.,

$$\mathbb{P}_i = \frac{e^{-\epsilon_i\beta}}{\sum\limits_i e^{-\epsilon_i\beta}}$$

This is in terms of energy.

Rewrite this in terms of velocity using

$$E = \frac{1}{2} mv^2 = \frac{1}{2}(v_x^2 + v_y^2 + v_z^2)$$

$$\begin{align*}
\mathbb{P}(v_x, v_y, v_z)dv_xdv_ydv_z &= \frac{e^{-\frac{m\beta}{2}(v_x^2+v_y^2+v_z^2)}dv_xdv_ydv_z}{\sum\limits_x\sum\limits_y\sum\limits_z e^{-\frac{m\beta}{2}(v_x^2+v_y^2+v_z^2)}}\\
&= \frac{e^{-\frac{m\beta}{2}(v_x^2+v_y^2+v_z^2)}dv_xdv_ydv_z}{\left(\sum\limits_x e^{-\frac{m\beta}{2}v_x^2}\right)\left(\sum\limits_y e^{-\frac{m\beta}{2}v_y^2}\right)\left(\sum\limits_z e^{-\frac{m\beta}{2}v_z^2}\right)}\\
& \text{ \hspace{35pt} } \downarrow\\
& \text{ \hspace{10pt} } \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} e^{-\frac{m\beta}{2} v_x^2} dx = \left(\frac{2\pi }{m\beta}\right)^{\frac{1}{2}}\\
&=\left(\frac{m\beta}{2\pi }\right)^{3/2} e^{-\frac{mv^2\beta}{2}}dv_xdv_ydv_z\\
& \text{ \hspace{100pt} } \downarrow \text{ convert this to spherical polar coord.}\\
& \text{ \hspace{100pt} } d\theta d\phi dv \\
& \text{ \hspace{100pt} \& symmetric in } \theta \& \phi\\
&= \left(\frac{m\beta}{2\pi }\right)^{3/2} e^{-\frac{mv^2\beta}{2}}4\pi v^2 dv
\end{align*}$$

$$\boxed{\mathbb{P}(v)dv = \left(\frac{m\beta}{2\pi}\right)^{3/2} e^{-\frac{mv^2\beta}{2}}4\pi v^2 dv}$$
For a benefit of the reader, we should put the more useful alternative
form(we have not identified $\beta$ yet)
$$\boxed{\mathbb{P}(v)dv = \left(\frac{m}{2\pi k_BT}\right)^{3/2} e^{-\frac{mv^2}{2k_BT}}4\pi v^2 dv}$$
