#  The Equipartition Principle

## Classical

> \"Each independent pure quadratic momentum or position term in the
> classical energy of a particle contribute $\frac{RT}{2}$ to the
> thermodynamic internal energy per mole.\"

Consider diatomic molecule having harmonic oscillator,

$$KE = \frac{p^2}{2\mu} \hspace{10pt} V = \frac{k_0r^2}{2}$$

Using integral expression (single degree of freedom),

$$\begin{align*}
Z &= \frac{1}{h}\int_{-\infty}^{\infty}\int_{-\infty}^{\infty}\exp\left[-\frac{p^2}{2\mu k_BT}-\frac{k_0r^2}{2k_BT}\right]dpdr\\
&\downarrow \text{ using table}\\
&= \frac{1}{h}\sqrt{2\pi \mu k_BT}\cdot\sqrt{\frac{2\pi}{k_0}k_BT}\\
&\text{\hspace{40pt}} \uparrow \text{\hspace{40pt}} \uparrow\\
&\text{\hspace{10pt} \begin{tabular}{c}momentum\\ part\end{tabular} \begin{tabular}{c}position\\ part\end{tabular}}
\end{align*}$$

Please see appendix for the origin of Plank constant if you are
interested.Note that use of integration is justifed because classical
treatment allows continous energy in any modes . In general (either
classical or quantum),\
$$\ln Z = \frac{1}{2}\ln T + \text{ const }$$

for pure quadratic term $p^2$ & $r^2$.

$$\frac{u}{k_BT} = T\left(\frac{\partial \ln Z}{\partial T}\right)_V = \frac{1}{2}$$

$$u= \frac{1}{2}k_BT \text{ for such term }$$

$\star$ $q_\text{vib}$\
from Physical Chemistry Part I,
$\epsilon_\text{vib} = \left(\nu + \frac{1}{2}\right) \hbar\omega$

$$\begin{align*}
q_\text{vib} &= \sum\limits_\nu e^{-\epsilon_\text{vib}/k_BT}\\
&=\sum\limits_\nu e^{-\left(\nu +\frac{1}{2}\right)\hbar\omega/k_BT}\\
&=e^{-\frac{\hbar\omega}{2k_BT}}\underbrace{\sum\limits_\nu e^{-\nu \hbar\omega/k_BT}}_{\text{geometric series}}\\
&=e^{-\frac{\hbar\omega}{2k_BT}}\frac{1}{1-e^{-\hbar\omega/k_BT}}
\end{align*}$$

Let $\Theta_\text{vib}:=\frac{\hbar\omega}{k_B}$ be vibrational
temperature. Then,

$$q_\text{vib} = \frac{e^{-\Theta_\text{vib}/2T}}{1-e^{-\Theta_\text{vib}/T}}$$

Here, we used geometric series instead of integration because of the
large step size for the vibrational energy as explained in Part I of
this textbook.\
